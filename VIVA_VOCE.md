# 🎓 Viva Voce — PhD Oral Defence

> **A complete reference for PhD viva preparation**
> The viva voce is a 1–3 hour oral examination of your completed thesis with two examiners. It is the most intellectually demanding assessment of doctoral study — and the one most candidates are least prepared for. Most students pass. The goal is not to catch you out; it is to verify that the work is genuinely yours, that you understand it completely, and that you can situate your contribution in the field.

## 1. What the Viva Actually Is

---

### 1.1 The Purpose

A viva serves three purposes that examiners hold simultaneously:

1. **Verification** — confirming that the thesis is genuinely your own work and that you understand it in full
2. **Examination** — assessing whether you can defend your methodological and theoretical choices under scrutiny
3. **Scholarly conversation** — engaging you as an emerging peer in the field

Most candidates over-prepare for the third and under-prepare for the second. The viva is not a research seminar where you present interesting ideas — it is an examination where every claim you make can be interrogated.

---

### 1.2 The Process

- **Duration:** typically 1.5–3 hours, occasionally longer for complex interdisciplinary theses
- **Examiners:** usually one internal (from your institution) and one external (a specialist in your field from another institution); sometimes two externals
- **Format:** conversational but structured; examiners usually work through the thesis chapter by chapter, following a list of questions prepared in advance
- **Outcome:** decided immediately after the viva in a brief examiner discussion; communicated to you the same day

---

### 1.3 Possible Outcomes

| Outcome | What it means |
|---------|--------------|
| **Pass without corrections** | Rare — the thesis is accepted as submitted |
| **Minor corrections** | Most common outcome — small changes required within 3 months (typically: clarifications, additional references, typographical errors, a paragraph or two) |
| **Major corrections** | Substantial revisions required within 6–12 months — often rewriting sections, additional analysis, restructuring chapters |
| **Resubmission with re-examination** | A second viva required after significant revision — uncommon but not catastrophic |
| **Fail** | Extremely rare in a viva a supervisor has approved for submission |

> 📝 **Tip:** Most PhD candidates receive minor corrections. Do not interpret a corrections requirement as failure — it is the standard outcome. Examiners often use corrections as a way of continuing a productive scholarly conversation.

## 2. Preparing Your Thesis

---

### 2.1 Re-reading as an Examiner

The most important preparation task is re-reading your own thesis — all of it — as if you have never seen it before, looking for:

- Claims you make without sufficient evidence
- Methodological decisions that are described but not justified
- Places where the argument is unclear, inconsistent, or assumes too much
- Literature you cite without engaging critically
- Limitations you have understated or omitted
- Moments where you hedge excessively — which signals uncertainty an examiner will probe

> 💡 **Key question:** For every significant claim in your thesis, can you defend it under direct questioning? If there is a claim you hope an examiner will not notice, that is exactly where they will probe.

---

### 2.2 Annotating Your Thesis

As you re-read, annotate:

- Post-it note the beginning of each chapter with a one-sentence summary of its argument
- Mark passages you feel uncertain about — and prepare a response
- Mark passages you are proud of — these are where you can speak with confidence and enthusiasm
- Note any literature published since you submitted that is relevant — knowing the field has moved is important even if you cannot incorporate it

---

### 2.3 Knowing Your Contribution

The most important thing you must be able to articulate is your original contribution. Practise answering this out loud:

*"My thesis contributes [X] to the field of [Y] because [Z] — and this was not previously available because [W]."*

This should take no longer than two minutes and should be specific. "I add to the literature on disability" is not a contribution. "I provide the first qualitative account of how autistic women experience diagnostic disclosure in primary care settings in Canada, which fills a gap in the predominantly quantitative, UK-focused literature" is.

## 3. The Typical Structure

Most vivas follow a recognisable arc, though examiners vary in their approach:

---

### 3.1 Opening

**"Please summarise your thesis and explain what you see as its main contribution."**

This is almost always the first question, in some form. Your answer should be:

- Clear and confident — 2–3 minutes
- Structured: research question → method → key findings → contribution
- In plain language — not hedged or over-qualified

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Beginning your summary with extensive background on why the field is important. Examiners know the field — get to your study quickly.

---

### 3.2 Chapter-by-Chapter Scrutiny

Examiners work through the thesis systematically, with prepared questions on each chapter. Typical focus areas:

| Chapter | What examiners look for |
|---------|------------------------|
| **Introduction** | Clarity of research question; rationale; scope |
| **Literature review** | Coverage; critical engagement; gap justification |
| **Methodology** | Justification of every choice; epistemological coherence; ethics |
| **Results / findings** | Accuracy; whether claims are proportionate to data |
| **Discussion** | Depth of interpretation; connection to literature; implications |
| **Conclusion** | Clarity of contribution; future directions |

---

### 3.3 Closing

Most vivas end with:

- "Is there anything you would change if you were starting again?"
- "Where do you see this work going next?"
- "Is there anything you would like to add?"

These are opportunities, not traps. Answer the first honestly and confidently — acknowledging what you would do differently demonstrates intellectual maturity. Use the last to make any point you feel has not been covered.

---

## 4. Question Types & How to Answer

---

### 4.1 The Contribution Question

*"What is new here? What does the field know now that it did not before your thesis?"*

Prepare a precise, confident answer. Have examples ready — specific findings, theoretical moves, or methodological contributions that are original.

---

### 4.2 The Defence Question

*"Why did you use X method rather than Y? Why did you choose this theoretical framework?"*

Every methodological and theoretical choice must be defensible. Your answer should:

1. Name what you chose
2. Explain why it was appropriate for your research question
3. Acknowledge what you might have missed by not using the alternative
4. Explain why the limitation does not undermine your findings

> 📝 **Tip:** Examiners are not looking for the "right" answer — they are looking for evidence that you made informed, reasoned decisions. "I chose this because it was appropriate for my question, and I considered the alternative but..." is a strong answer.

---

### 4.3 The Limitation Question

*"What are the limitations of your study? What would you do differently?"*

Do not minimise your limitations — this looks defensive and unconvincing. Do not catastrophise them either — they should not undermine the whole thesis. Strike the balance:

1. Name the limitation precisely
2. Explain why it arose (design choice, access constraints, resource limits)
3. Explain what it means for the scope of your claims — not that it invalidates them, but where it draws the boundary

---

### 4.4 The Challenge Question

*"Author X argues the opposite of what you claim in Chapter 3 — how do you respond?"*

This tests whether you know the field well enough to engage with its debates.

- Pause — it is acceptable to take a few seconds
- Acknowledge what the cited author argues accurately and charitably
- Explain the specific point of difference
- Defend your position with reference to your data or theoretical framework
- If the challenge has merit, acknowledge it: "That is a real tension — my response to it is..."

> 💡 **Key question:** Is there a paper in your field that directly challenges your central argument? You should know it, be able to summarise it accurately, and have a prepared response.

---

### 4.5 When You Do Not Know

It is acceptable to say: "I don't know the answer to that off the top of my head" or "I would need to think about that more carefully." What matters is what comes next — a genuine attempt to reason through the question, not silence or deflection.

## 5. Corrections

---

### 5.1 Responding to Minor Corrections

Minor corrections (the most common outcome) typically include:

- Clarifying an argument or claim
- Adding or updating references
- Correcting typographical or formatting errors
- Adding a paragraph addressing a limitation more fully

Approach them as an intellectual task, not a punishment. Read the examiners' feedback carefully, complete the corrections methodically, and resubmit within the specified period. Your internal examiner typically signs off minor corrections.

---

### 5.2 Responding to Major Corrections

Major corrections require more substantial revision — potentially rewriting sections, conducting additional analysis, or restructuring chapters. This feels discouraging but is recoverable. The thesis remains fundamentally yours; the corrections improve it.

- Meet with your supervisor as soon as possible to understand the scope
- Create a structured revision plan before beginning
- Keep records of every change you make for the final submission

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Preparation Timeline

| Timing | Preparation |
|--------|------------|
| 6–8 weeks before | Read your thesis in full as a critical examiner; annotate |
| 4–6 weeks before | Prepare answers to likely questions; practise out loud |
| 2–4 weeks before | Schedule mock viva with supervisor or peers |
| 1–2 weeks before | Final read-through; prepare your contribution statement |
| Week before | Do not cram — review key points; ensure you are rested |

### ✍️ The Mock Viva

A mock viva with your supervisor (and ideally a peer) is the single most valuable preparation activity. The experience of speaking your arguments aloud under questioning is irreplaceable — the gap between having thoughts and articulating them precisely under pressure is larger than you expect.

Ask your mock viva panel to:

- Ask the opening question without advance notice of their exact phrasing
- Challenge claims you make in your answers
- Push back when your answer is vague or hedged
- Ask the "what would you do differently" question

---

### ✅ Preparation Checklist

- [ ] Full thesis re-read with examiner's eyes — uncertain passages annotated
- [ ] Contribution statement prepared and rehearsed (2 minutes, out loud)
- [ ] Methodological choices defensible — justified, not just described
- [ ] Key limitations identified and responses prepared
- [ ] Five to ten recent papers in the field read that postdate the thesis
- [ ] The most likely challenge to your central argument identified and a response prepared
- [ ] Mock viva completed
- [ ] Post-mock weaknesses addressed in further preparation
- [ ] Thesis copy annotated and tabbed for easy navigation in the room
- [ ] Administrative logistics confirmed: location, time, examiner names

---

### 🎓 Final Note

The viva is the moment when four or more years of work is examined — but it is also the moment you are welcomed into a scholarly community as a peer. Most examiners enter the room hoping you will pass. They have read your thesis carefully and, if it has reached the viva, your supervisor believes it is ready.

The examination is serious. But it is also, for many candidates, the best and most engaged conversation about their work they will ever have — with two people who have read every word of it. Approach it with that in mind.
