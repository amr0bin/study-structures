# 📋 Annotated Bibliography

> **A complete reference for postgraduate annotated bibliographies**
> An annotated bibliography is a curated, critically evaluated list of sources. Each entry does two things: it describes what the source says, and it assesses what the source is worth. The critical evaluation is what separates it from a reading list.

## 1. What an Annotated Bibliography Is For

> **Typical assignment:** 8–15 sources · 150–300 words per annotation · total 1,500–4,000 words

An annotated bibliography is often assigned early in a programme to develop the skill of reading critically — evaluating sources rather than simply consuming them. It is the precursor to literature synthesis: you cannot synthesise a field you have not first learned to evaluate piece by piece.

At postgraduate level, the emphasis is on critical evaluation. Description alone (summarising what the source argues) earns moderate marks. Critical evaluation (assessing quality, situating the source in the field, and justifying its relevance to your work) is what distinguishes a strong annotation.

> 💡 **Key question:** After reading your annotation, would someone know not just *what* this source argues but *how reliable it is*, *what it contributes*, and *why you chose it*?

## 2. Selecting Sources

Before writing a single annotation, the selection of sources is itself an intellectual act.

---

### 2.1 What belongs in a postgraduate annotated bibliography

- **Peer-reviewed journal articles** — the primary currency of academic argument
- **Scholarly monographs** — books by recognised researchers, published by academic presses
- **Foundational theoretical texts** — the original source of key frameworks you are using
- **High-quality policy documents or grey literature** — where directly relevant, with careful critical attention to their limitations
- **Recent empirical studies** — ideally within the last ten years, unless the older work is foundational

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Websites, blogs, Wikipedia, newspaper articles, or sources without clear authorship or peer-review process — unless the assignment specifically requires non-academic sources and you discuss their limitations explicitly.

---

### 2.2 Organising your selection

Most annotated bibliographies are organised alphabetically by author surname (APA convention). Some assignments ask for thematic organisation — check the brief.

- Aim for a balance of theoretical and empirical sources where both exist in your field
- Include sources that represent different positions in any debate — not just sources that support your existing view
- If two sources say essentially the same thing, choose the more rigorous, recent, or widely cited one

## 3. Per-Entry Structure

Each entry has two components: the full reference and the annotation.

---

### 3.1 The Full Reference

Always formatted in APA 7th (unless your programme specifies otherwise), before the annotation begins.

**Journal article format:**
Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article. *Title of Journal*, *volume*(issue), page–page. https://doi.org/xxxxx

**Book format:**
Author, A. A. (Year). *Title of work: Capital letter also for subtitle*. Publisher.

**Chapter in edited book:**
Author, A. A. (Year). Title of chapter. In E. E. Editor (Ed.), *Title of book* (pp. page–page). Publisher.

> 📝 **Tip:** Use a reference manager (Zotero is free, excellent, and produces APA 7th automatically). Do not format references manually — formatting errors cost marks and waste time.

---

### 3.2 The Annotation — Four Components

| Component | What it does | Typical length |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Summary** | What the source argues or reports | 2–3 sentences |
| **Critical evaluation** | Methodological quality, limitations, credibility | 2–3 sentences |
| **Situating in the field** | How it relates to other work, debates, or positions | 1–2 sentences |
| **Relevance** | Why you chose it for *your* work specifically | 1–2 sentences |

---

## 4. Writing the Annotation

---

### 4.1 The Summary

Describe the source's main argument, methodology (if empirical), and key findings or conclusions. This should be in your own words — no direct quotation.

- For theoretical texts: what is the central argument or framework?
- For empirical studies: what was the research question, how was it investigated, and what did it find?
- For policy documents: what position does it take and what does it recommend?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Beginning with "This article argues..." or "In this book, the author..." — these are weak openers. Begin with the substance: "Drawing on interviews with 40 autistic adults, Smith (2021) demonstrates that..."

---

### 4.2 The Critical Evaluation

This is where most annotations at postgraduate level fall short. Description without evaluation is not critical engagement.

Questions to ask of any source:

- **Sample and generalisability:** Who does this research represent, and who does it exclude?
- **Methodology:** Is the method appropriate to the question? Are there significant design limitations?
- **Theoretical assumptions:** What does the source take for granted? What lens does it use, and what does that lens obscure?
- **Evidence quality:** Are claims proportionate to the evidence? Is the reasoning sound?
- **Date and currency:** Is this still the best available evidence on this question?
- **Positionality:** Who funded this research? Does the authors' disciplinary background or institutional affiliation shape the findings?

> 💡 **Key question:** Would you cite this source in your own work without qualification? If you would qualify it ("although limited to a Western, educated sample..."), that qualification belongs in the annotation.

---

### 4.3 Situating in the Field

Show that you understand how this source relates to the broader literature.

- Does it confirm, challenge, or extend other work you have read?
- Is it foundational — work that established a concept or framework that others build on?
- Is it contested — a study whose conclusions are disputed in the field?
- Does it represent a particular theoretical tradition or methodological approach?

---

### 4.4 Relevance to Your Work

The final element makes the annotation specific to *your* project rather than generically useful.

- Why does this source matter for your specific research question or essay?
- What does it contribute that other sources in your bibliography do not?
- Will you use it for its theoretical framework, its empirical findings, its methodology, or its argument?

---

### 4.5 A Complete Example

> Gillies, V., & Robinson, Y. (2012). Including 'hard to reach' parents: Promise and problems. *Symbolic Interaction*, *35*(4), 529–544. https://doi.org/10.1002/symb.36
>
> Drawing on ethnographic fieldwork in two UK family support programmes, Gillies and Robinson examine how the discourse of 'hard to reach' constructs deficit narratives around working-class parents while obscuring structural barriers to participation. The study is methodologically rigorous, with sustained fieldwork across two sites and careful reflexive attention to the researchers' own positioning. Its limitation is geographic scope — findings reflect UK policy contexts that may not transfer directly to other welfare state configurations. This source sits within a body of critical sociological work on parenting policy (cf. Lareau, 2003; Vincent & Ball, 2006) and represents one of the stronger empirical challenges to deficit-based family intervention models. For my essay on asset-based approaches in disability support, this source provides the critical theoretical grounding for my argument that participation-focused frameworks must first dismantle the discursive construction of non-participation as individual failure.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Recommended Order

| Order | Step | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | **Search and screen** | Collect more sources than you need, then select the best |
| 2 | **Read actively** | Take notes on argument, method, limitations as you go |
| 3 | **Format references first** | Get the bibliography structure right before writing annotations |
| 4 | **Draft summaries** | Establish what each source says before evaluating it |
| 5 | **Add critical evaluation** | Return to each entry with evaluative questions |
| 6 | **Add field situation and relevance** | Show how the sources relate to each other and to your work |
| 7 | **Revise for consistency** | Each annotation should be roughly the same length and depth |

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] All references formatted correctly in APA 7th (or specified style)
- [ ] Sources are peer-reviewed and appropriate for postgraduate level
- [ ] Selection represents a range of positions and evidence types
- [ ] Each annotation includes summary, critical evaluation, field situation, and relevance
- [ ] Summary is in your own words — no direct quotation
- [ ] Critical evaluation goes beyond description to assess quality and limitations
- [ ] Relevance connects specifically to *your* research question or essay
- [ ] Annotations are roughly consistent in length and depth
- [ ] Organisation is alphabetical (or thematic if specified)
- [ ] Total word count is within the permitted range

---

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
Often assigned as preparation for the dissertation literature review. Choose sources that you expect to use in your thesis — this assignment is an opportunity to build your bibliography while developing your critical reading skills simultaneously.

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
Look for sources across both quantitative (RCT, meta-analysis) and qualitative (phenomenological, grounded theory) traditions. Critical evaluation should address not just methodological quality but also what the method can and cannot reveal about therapeutic experience.

#### Diploma (e.g. Art Therapy)
Include arts-based research sources and foundational art therapy texts alongside empirical literature. The critical evaluation of practitioner-research sources should address reflexivity and the dual role of practitioner and researcher.

#### PhD
At PhD level, an annotated bibliography may be required for a specific literature domain as part of your training. The evaluations should demonstrate command of the field — knowing which studies are foundational, which are contested, and which represent methodological advances.
