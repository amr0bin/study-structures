# ✍️ Critical & Analytical Essay

> **A complete reference for postgraduate essay writing**
> The most frequent assignment type across all taught modules. The structure is deceptively simple — introduction, argument, conclusion — but the quality lives in the precision of your claim, the rigour of your evidence, and the honesty of your engagement with opposing views.

## 1. Introduction

> **Typical length:** 10% of total word count
> *(1,500-word essay: ~150 words · 5,000-word essay: ~500 words)*

The introduction does three things in order: it orients the reader to the territory, it identifies the specific question or tension you are addressing, and it states your argument — your thesis statement — clearly. Everything after it is the defence of that claim.

---

### 1.1 Context and Territory

Open by establishing the broader field, debate, or problem your essay enters. This is not a history of the topic — it is the minimum context a reader needs to understand why your question matters.

- Two to four sentences is usually sufficient; do not bury your argument under background
- Establish the stakes: who is affected, what is at stake, why this debate matters now
- Move from broad to specific — funnel toward your exact question

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Opening with dictionary definitions ("According to Merriam-Webster, disability is...") or sweeping historical overviews. Start as close to your argument as possible.

---

### 1.2 The Thesis Statement

The single most important sentence in your essay. It is your answer to the question — a specific, debatable, arguable claim that the rest of the essay will defend.

- State it clearly and early — by the end of your introduction
- It must be **arguable**: a claim that someone could reasonably disagree with
- It must be **specific**: not "social media affects mental health" but *how*, *for whom*, and *under what conditions*
- It should map onto your essay's structure — a reader should be able to predict your main sections from it

> 💡 **Key question:** Could someone write a counter-essay to your thesis statement? If not, it is not argumentative enough — it is a statement of fact, not a claim.

> 📝 **Tip:** Write your thesis statement before you write anything else. Then write the essay to defend it. If the essay you write no longer matches the thesis, revise the thesis — not the essay.

---

### 1.3 Roadmap (optional but often valuable)

A one to two sentence indication of how the essay will proceed. Not required in shorter essays but helpful in anything over 3,000 words.

- State your main sections or moves in the order they appear
- Use the same language you will use in your headings or topic sentences
- This is functional, not decorative — skip it if it adds nothing

## 2. Main Body

> **Typical length:** 75–80% of total word count

The body is where your argument is built. Each paragraph makes one claim, supports it with evidence, analyses what the evidence shows, and connects it back to your thesis. This is the PEEL or PEAL structure: **Point — Evidence — Analysis — Link**.

---

### 2.1 Paragraph Structure

Every body paragraph should be able to answer these four questions:

- **What is this paragraph claiming?** (Your topic sentence — the paragraph's argument in one sentence)
- **What is the evidence?** (Source, data, example, quotation — cited correctly)
- **What does the evidence show?** (Your analysis — not what it says, but what it *means*)
- **Why does this matter for your overall argument?** (The link back to your thesis)

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Paragraphs that are entirely quotation and paraphrase with no analysis. A paragraph that summarises what three authors said has not made an argument — it has assembled a reading list.

---

### 2.2 Using Evidence

Evidence in a postgraduate essay means peer-reviewed literature, primary sources, empirical data, or well-established theory. How you use it matters as much as what you cite.

| Evidence type | What it does | What it cannot do |
|--------------|-------------|------------------|
| Quotation | Captures precise wording where exact language matters | Substitute for your own analysis |
| Paraphrase | Shows you understand the source; integrates smoothly | Conceal that you haven't engaged critically |
| Statistical data | Grounds abstract claims in empirical reality | Prove causation without careful qualification |
| Theoretical claim | Establishes the framework through which you interpret | Stand alone without application to your argument |

- Quote sparingly — paraphrase unless the exact wording is what matters
- Every citation needs analysis: *what does this evidence mean for your argument?*
- Engage critically with sources — acknowledge their limitations

> 📝 **Tip:** After writing each body paragraph, read only the first and last sentences. Do they form a coherent logical unit — claim and conclusion? If not, the analysis in the middle is not doing its job.

---

### 2.3 Organising Your Paragraphs

The sequence of your paragraphs constitutes the logic of your argument. Several organisational approaches work depending on your essay type:

| Structure | When to use it |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Strongest to weakest argument** | When you have several independent supporting claims |
| **Thematic** | When the essay covers distinct sub-topics that each support the thesis |
| **Chronological / developmental** | When the argument depends on showing a historical progression |
| **Dialectical** | When you are working through a tension between two positions |

- Use transition sentences between paragraphs to show the logical connection, not just sequence
- Each paragraph should feel *necessary* — if removing it doesn't weaken your argument, cut it

## 3. Counter-argument

> **Typical length:** 10–15% of total word count
> *Usually one substantial paragraph, sometimes two*

Engaging honestly with the strongest objection to your argument is not weakness — it is intellectual maturity. An essay that ignores opposition looks either unaware of it or afraid of it. Examiners at postgraduate level expect you to know what could be said against you.

---

### 3.1 Structure of a Counter-argument Paragraph

1. **Name the opposing position accurately and charitably** — do not set up a weak version to knock down easily (this is the "straw man" fallacy)
2. **Explain why someone might reasonably hold it** — cite scholars or evidence that supports it
3. **Identify the specific limitation or flaw** — what does this position miss, oversimplify, or assume?
4. **Show why your argument holds despite this objection** — or how it accommodates the objection

> 💡 **Key question:** Is the objection you are engaging with the *strongest* possible objection to your thesis? If there is a better one that you have not addressed, an examiner will find it.

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Summarising an opposing view and then simply asserting that you disagree. "Some scholars argue X, but I believe Y" is not a rebuttal — it is a disagreement without argument.

---

## 4. Conclusion

> **Typical length:** 10% of total word count

The conclusion does not introduce new evidence or new arguments. It synthesises what the essay has demonstrated and states — clearly, confidently — what this means.

---

### 4.1 What the Conclusion Must Do

1. **Restate your thesis** — not word-for-word, but in light of everything the essay has argued; it should feel earned now
2. **Synthesise your main points** — not a list of what each section said, but how they work together to support your claim
3. **State the wider significance** — what does your argument mean beyond the essay itself? Theoretical implications, practical applications, questions it opens

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Conclusions that begin "In conclusion, I have argued that..." and then repeat the essay in compressed form. Synthesis is not summary — it is the so-what.

> 📝 **Tip:** Read your introduction and your conclusion back-to-back. They should form a coherent unit — the conclusion delivering on the promise of the introduction.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Recommended Writing Order

| Order | Step | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | **Write the thesis statement** | Everything follows from this |
| 2 | **Plan the paragraph sequence** | Test the logic before committing to prose |
| 3 | **Draft the body first** | This is where the argument lives |
| 4 | **Draft the conclusion** | You now know what you have argued |
| 5 | **Draft the introduction last** | You can only introduce what you have written |
| 6 | **Revise for argument coherence** | Does every paragraph serve the thesis? |
| 7 | **Revise for prose clarity** | Separate pass — don't do both at once |

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Thesis statement is clear, specific, and arguable — stated in the introduction
- [ ] Every body paragraph has a topic sentence that makes a claim
- [ ] Every piece of evidence is followed by analysis, not just paraphrase
- [ ] Every paragraph connects back to the thesis
- [ ] Counter-argument engages with the strongest possible objection
- [ ] Counter-argument is rebutted, not just named
- [ ] Conclusion synthesises rather than summarises
- [ ] Conclusion states the wider significance of the argument
- [ ] Introduction and conclusion form a coherent matched pair
- [ ] No new evidence or arguments introduced in the conclusion
- [ ] All sources cited correctly in APA 7th (or required style)
- [ ] Word count is within the permitted range

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
Theoretical engagement is central — you will often be asked to apply a specific framework to a problem or debate. The quality of your engagement with theory (not just citing it, but using it analytically) is what distinguishes a strong MSc essay.

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
Essays often require integration of theory and practice. Your thesis statement will frequently take a position on how a theoretical framework applies to, limits, or illuminates clinical practice. Reflexivity about your own position is often expected.

#### Diploma (e.g. Art Therapy)
Essays tend to be shorter and more focused on practitioner application. The counter-argument section is an opportunity to demonstrate that you understand the field's internal debates — not just the consensus view.

#### PhD
At PhD level, essays and seminar papers are where you develop and test arguments that will eventually feed into your thesis. Treat every essay as a contribution to your scholarly argument, not a standalone exercise.
