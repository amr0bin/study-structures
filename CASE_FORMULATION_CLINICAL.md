# 🧠 Case Formulation — Clinical & Counselling

> **A complete reference for MA Counselling Psychology and PhD Clinical Psychology**
> A case formulation is a structured clinical analysis that integrates what a client presents with what theory explains about that presentation — leading to a coherent, evidence-based intervention plan. It is both a clinical tool and a demonstration of theoretical competence.

## 1. Presenting Problem

> **Typical length:** 10–15% of total word count

Begin with the client's own account. The presenting problem is what brings them now — in their words, not yet filtered through theory. This section establishes the clinical picture before interpretation begins.

---

### 1.1 The Client's Account

- What does the client say is the problem? Use their language, not clinical terminology
- When did the difficulty begin, or when did it become acute?
- What has prompted them to seek help at this particular point in time?
- How does the problem affect their daily life — relationships, work, sense of self?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Introducing theoretical interpretation in this section. The presenting problem should be described, not yet explained. The explanation comes in the formulation.

---

### 1.2 Mental State Presentation

Note the relevant aspects of the client's presentation as observed or reported:

- Affect: what emotional states are present, and how are they expressed?
- Cognition: any notable patterns of thought, beliefs about self, world, or future?
- Behaviour: what is the client doing or avoiding as a result of the difficulty?
- Somatic: any relevant physical symptoms or experiences?

---

### 1.3 Relevant Symptom Picture

If relevant to your formulation, briefly note whether the presentation maps onto any recognised diagnostic categories — without reducing the person to a diagnosis.

> 📝 **Tip:** In Counselling Psychology especially, the relationship between diagnosis and formulation is contested. Your programme will have a position on this — follow it. In general, a formulation should illuminate more than a diagnosis and should not subordinate the person's experience to a category.

## 2. Developmental & Social History

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count

This section provides the historical and contextual background necessary to understand the client's current presentation. The selection of what to include is itself an interpretive act — guided by your theoretical framework.

---

### 2.1 Early History

- Family of origin: significant relationships, attachment experiences, early losses or disruptions
- Developmental experiences: school, peer relationships, early identity formation
- Significant adverse experiences: trauma, loss, abuse, neglect — handled with care and appropriate clinical language

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** A biographical chronology that simply lists events without connecting them to the presenting problem. Every piece of history should be included because it helps explain *how the client came to present this way*.

---

### 2.2 Current Context

- Significant relationships: quality, stability, sources of support or stress
- Social and material circumstances: housing, employment, finances, community
- Cultural, ethnic, and identity factors: how do these shape the client's experience and the meaning they make of difficulty?
- Previous contact with services: therapy, psychiatry, social work — what helped, what didn't?

---

### 2.3 Predisposing Factors

Drawing on the history, identify the factors that made this client vulnerable to the current difficulties:

- Biological: any relevant genetic, neurological, or physiological factors
- Psychological: early attachment patterns, core beliefs formed in childhood, developmental difficulties
- Social: chronic stressors, social isolation, structural disadvantage, cultural factors

> 📝 **Tip:** Predisposing factors are not blame — they are the conditions that created vulnerability. Frame them clinically and humanely, not as deficits.

---

## 3. Theoretical Formulation

> **Typical length:** 20–25% of total word count
> *The intellectual centrepiece of the case formulation*

This section applies your chosen theoretical framework to the case — explaining how the framework accounts for the client's presentation, what it highlights, and what it might miss.

---

### 3.1 Naming and Applying the Framework

State your theoretical orientation explicitly and apply it systematically to the case.

| Common frameworks | What they focus on |
|------------------|-------------------|
| **Cognitive-behavioural (CBT)** | Thought-feeling-behaviour cycles; cognitive distortions; behavioural avoidance |
| **Psychodynamic / psychoanalytic** | Unconscious processes; defence mechanisms; relational patterns rooted in early experience |
| **Person-centred** | Conditions of worth; incongruence between self-concept and experience; the therapeutic relationship as curative |
| **Attachment theory** | Internal working models; attachment styles; relational patterns across contexts |
| **ACT / third wave CBT** | Psychological flexibility; experiential avoidance; values-based action |
| **Systemic** | Relational and family systems; patterns of interaction; context as the unit of analysis |
| **Integrative** | Drawing explicitly on multiple frameworks — requires clear rationale for integration |

- Explain how the framework accounts for the specific features of *this* client's presentation
- Show how history, presenting problem, and current context fit together through this lens
- Acknowledge what the framework does not easily explain

> 💡 **Key question:** Could another formulation, using a different theoretical framework, account for this presentation equally well? If so, what is your rationale for choosing this one?

---

### 3.2 Core Beliefs and Patterns (if applicable)

Many frameworks identify central psychological structures that maintain the client's difficulty:

- **Core beliefs** (CBT): deeply held beliefs about self, others, and the world formed in early experience
- **Internal working models** (attachment): templates for relationship based on early attachment experiences
- **Object relations** (psychodynamic): internalised relational patterns that shape current relationships
- **Rules for living**: conditional assumptions that protect core beliefs

## 4. Precipitating & Perpetuating Factors

> **Typical length:** 10% of total word count

---

### 4.1 Precipitating Factors

What triggered the current episode or brought the difficulties to a head?

- A specific life event (loss, transition, conflict, failure)
- An accumulation of stressors reaching a tipping point
- A change in circumstances that removed a previously stabilising factor

---

### 4.2 Perpetuating Factors

What is maintaining the problem — preventing natural recovery?

- Cognitive: thoughts and beliefs that keep the cycle going (e.g., avoidance maintained by catastrophic predictions)
- Behavioural: safety behaviours, avoidance, or compensatory strategies that provide short-term relief but maintain long-term difficulty
- Interpersonal: relational patterns that reinforce the problem
- Social / systemic: structural factors (poverty, discrimination, social isolation) that perpetuate distress

> 📝 **Tip:** Perpetuating factors are often where the intervention levers are. Understanding what maintains the problem points toward what needs to change.

---

## 5. Protective Factors & Strengths

> **Typical length:** 5–8% of total word count

A formulation that focuses only on deficits and difficulties is incomplete. Protective factors and strengths are clinically important — they shape the therapeutic approach, inform what resources can be built on, and affect prognosis.

- Personal strengths: resilience, insight, capacity for reflection, creativity, humour
- Relational resources: supportive relationships, community belonging, secure attachments
- Cultural and spiritual resources: community, faith, cultural identity as a source of meaning and support
- Previous coping: what has the client managed before, and how?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Formulaic strengths lists that do not connect to the individual client. "The client has good social skills" is not a clinical finding — "the client maintains two close friendships despite social anxiety, suggesting capacity for intimacy when safe enough" is.

## 6. Intervention Plan

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count

The intervention plan follows directly from the formulation. If the formulation is accurate, the intervention should be self-evident.

---

### 6.1 Goals

State the therapeutic goals in concrete, achievable terms — ideally co-constructed with the client.

- **Overarching goal:** what the client and therapist are working toward
- **Intermediate goals:** the steps toward the overarching goal
- Frame goals positively (moving toward something) rather than purely as symptom reduction

---

### 6.2 Therapeutic Modality and Rationale

- What therapeutic approach will you use, and why is it indicated for this client and this presentation?
- What does the evidence say about this approach for presentations of this kind?
- What specific techniques or interventions are indicated?
- How does the formulation guide your choice — not just the diagnosis?

---

### 6.3 Therapeutic Relationship Considerations

- What relational patterns (from the formulation) might emerge in the therapeutic relationship?
- How will you use the therapeutic relationship therapeutically?
- What challenges might arise, and how will you approach them?

> 📝 **Tip:** In Counselling Psychology, the therapeutic relationship is often considered the primary agent of change — not the technique. Your intervention plan should address the relationship explicitly, not just the method.

---

### 6.4 Prognosis

An honest, evidence-based estimate of likely outcomes and relevant factors:

- What factors suggest the client is likely to engage and benefit from treatment?
- What factors may complicate treatment or predict a more challenging course?

---

## 7. Risk Assessment

> **Typically a distinct section — never omitted**

Risk assessment is not a formality — it is a clinical and ethical responsibility. In a case formulation assignment, it demonstrates that you can hold clinical complexity and legal / ethical obligation simultaneously.

---

### 7.1 Risk to Self

- Suicidal ideation: presence, intensity, frequency, plan, intent, access to means
- Self-harm: current or historical, function, severity
- Neglect of physical health or safety

---

### 7.2 Risk to Others

- Any indicators of risk to identified or identifiable others
- Domestic violence or safeguarding concerns

---

### 7.3 Protective Factors Against Risk

- Reasons for living; future orientation
- Social support; therapeutic alliance
- Engagement with services

---

### 7.4 Risk Management Plan

- What monitoring is in place?
- What is the safety plan?
- When would escalation or referral be indicated?
- Who else is involved in the client's care?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Risk sections that simply state "low / medium / high" risk without evidence or management plan. At postgraduate level, risk must be analysed and managed, not just categorised.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Reflexivity — Your Positionality in the Formulation

In Counselling Psychology, reflexivity is not optional. Your theoretical orientation, personal history, cultural background, and relationship to the client's presenting problem all shape what you notice, how you interpret, and what you might miss.

Include a brief reflexive statement — either woven through the formulation or in a dedicated section, depending on your programme's requirements:

- What in your own background or identity might affect how you work with this client?
- What countertransference responses are you aware of?
- What assumptions might your chosen theoretical framework carry that could be limiting for this client?

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Presenting problem described in the client's own terms — no premature interpretation
- [ ] Developmental and social history is selective and purposeful — not biographical
- [ ] Theoretical framework is named, applied, and justified
- [ ] Framework is applied to *this* client specifically — not generic theory
- [ ] Predisposing factors connect history to current presentation
- [ ] Precipitating factors explain *why now*
- [ ] Perpetuating factors explain *what maintains* the difficulty
- [ ] Protective factors and strengths are specific and clinically meaningful
- [ ] Intervention plan follows from the formulation
- [ ] Therapeutic modality is evidenced and rationale is stated
- [ ] Therapeutic relationship is addressed in the intervention plan
- [ ] Risk is assessed, evidenced, and managed — not just categorised
- [ ] Reflexivity is addressed
- [ ] Client anonymised appropriately (if based on real client work)
- [ ] All clinical and theoretical sources cited in APA 7th

---

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
Reflexivity is central. The therapeutic relationship is foregrounded — not just the method. Your formulation should demonstrate that you are thinking about *this person*, not a diagnostic category.

#### PhD (e.g. Clinical Psychology)
At doctoral clinical level, formulations are often assessed for the sophistication of theoretical integration, the quality of the risk formulation, and the clinical reasoning that connects formulation to intervention. Evidence-based treatment selection is expected to be grounded in the research literature.
