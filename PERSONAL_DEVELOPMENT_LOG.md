# 📓 Personal Development Log & Process Journal

> **A complete reference for MA Counselling Psychology and Diploma Art Therapy**
> A personal development log is not a diary. It is a critically reflective document — maintained throughout a placement or training year — that tracks your development as a practitioner, processes your affective responses to the work, and connects experience to theory. It is assessed on depth of self-examination and quality of critical reflection, not on emotional disclosure alone.

## 1. What This Document Is and Is Not

---

### 1.1 What it is

A personal development log is a **critically reflective record** of your development as a practitioner across a training period. It is:

- **Ongoing** — written regularly throughout the year, not reconstructed at the end
- **Critical** — examining assumptions, patterns, and reactions, not just reporting them
- **Theoretically grounded** — connecting experience to the frameworks you are developing
- **Professionally purposeful** — oriented toward improving your practice and deepening your self-knowledge as a practitioner

---

### 1.2 What it is not

- A diary of what happened each day
- A confidential space where professional standards do not apply
- An opportunity to process difficulties with specific clients in ways that breach confidentiality
- A space for unfiltered emotional expression without critical examination

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Confusing emotional honesty with emotional disclosure. The log requires the former — naming your responses precisely and examining what they reveal — not the latter, which can become self-indulgent or clinically inappropriate.

---

## 2. Per-Entry Structure

Most programmes suggest a weekly or fortnightly entry rhythm. Each entry should address:

---

### 2.1 The Core Five Elements

| Element | What it captures | Typical length per entry |
|---------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Experience / observation** | What occurred — specific and concrete | 2–3 sentences |
| **Emotional and somatic response** | Your affective reaction, named precisely | 3–4 sentences |
| **Theoretical connection** | Which framework or concept illuminates this? | 3–5 sentences with citations |
| **Implications for practice** | What does this mean for how you work? | 2–3 sentences |
| **Supervision link** | What will you bring? What support do you need? | 1–2 sentences |

A complete entry of 250–400 words covering these five elements is more useful than a long, discursive diary entry that circles around experience without analysing it.

---

### 2.2 The Experience / Observation

Be specific. "I had a difficult session" is not a useful opening. "During a session with a client working through bereavement, I noticed a strong pull to offer reassurance at a moment when sitting with silence would have been more therapeutically appropriate" is.

- Name the specific moment, not a general impression of the day
- Use present tense where possible — it keeps the writing immediate
- Omit identifying details: clients are Client A, Client B; supervisors are referred to by role, not name

---

### 2.3 Emotional and Somatic Response

Name your feelings with precision and without censorship. The feelings that are hardest to name are usually the most clinically significant.

- What did you feel, and when exactly did you feel it?
- Where in your body did you notice the response?
- Were there feelings that surprised you, or that you were reluctant to acknowledge?
- Was there a gap between what you *presented* and what you *felt*?

> 💡 **Key question:** Are you naming the feeling you actually had, or the feeling that is more comfortable to report in an assessed document? The log is only useful if it is honest.

---

### 2.4 Theoretical Connection

Connect the experience to relevant theory, framework, or literature. This is what lifts the entry from journalling to critical reflection.

- Which concept or framework helps make sense of what happened?
- Does the theory account for your response, or does your experience complicate the theory?
- Cite the source — even in a personal log, intellectual honesty requires attribution

> 📝 **Tip:** You do not need to write a literature essay in each entry. One or two precise theoretical connections per entry, with citation, is sufficient and more useful than broad theoretical gesturing.

---

### 2.5 Implications for Practice

What does this experience mean for how you work?

- What would you do differently in a similar situation?
- What is this experience revealing about your developing therapeutic style, assumptions, or edges?
- What do you need to develop, learn, or practise?

---

### 2.6 Supervision Link

Identify what you will bring to supervision from this entry. The log and supervision should be in genuine dialogue — the log surfaces material, supervision processes it, and the log records what emerged.

---

## 3. The Five Reflective Moves

Think of each entry as moving through five positions:

| Move | The question | The register |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Observation** | What happened? | Factual, specific |
| **Sensation** | What did I feel? | Honest, somatic, precise |
| **Interpretation** | What does this mean? | Analytical, theoretical |
| **Positioning** | What does this reveal about me? | Reflexive, self-examining |
| **Forward movement** | What will I do with this? | Practical, professional |

An entry that stops at Observation and Sensation is a diary. An entry that moves through all five is critically reflective practice.

## 4. Tracking Development Over Time

The log's power as a document lies in what becomes visible when you read across entries over weeks and months.

---

### 4.1 Patterns to Watch For

- Recurring feelings or reactions in similar situations — what do they indicate?
- Clients or interactions you find yourself dreading, avoiding, or seeking — why?
- Moments where your practice felt most authentic — and most performed
- Shifts in your theoretical orientation as your experience deepens
- How your relationship to uncertainty, not-knowing, and difficulty is changing

> 📝 **Tip:** At the end of each month, spend 30 minutes reading back through your entries and writing a brief synthesis: what patterns are you noticing? This synthesis is often the most valuable part of the log for year-end review and submission.

---

### 4.2 The Year-End Review

Many programmes ask for a reflective summary as part of the portfolio submission. This is where the log proves its worth — a year of regular entries provides rich material for a considered account of your development.

A year-end summary should address:

1. Where you were at the beginning of the year — your anxieties, assumptions, and theoretical position
2. The significant experiences that challenged, deepened, or shifted your thinking
3. How your practice has developed — with specific examples from the log
4. Where you are now — what you understand about yourself as a practitioner that you could not have known at the start
5. What remains unresolved or in progress — professional development is not a finished project

## 5. For Art Therapy: The Process Journal

In Art Therapy training, the process journal is a specific assessed component that combines visual art-making with written reflection. It is described in more detail in the [Parallel Practice guide](PARALLEL_PRACTICE.html) but its key characteristics within the assessed log structure are:

---

### 5.1 What the Process Journal Contains

- **The artworks themselves** — made throughout training, in response to your therapeutic work, your own emotional process, and the course material
- **Written reflection on each piece** — what arose in the making, what surprised you, what the process evoked
- **Theoretical connections** — how art therapy theory illuminates your experience of making and of observing clients make

---

### 5.2 What Is Assessed

| Assessed element | What it demonstrates |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Engagement with making | Genuine, ongoing practice — not retrospective production |
| Reflexivity | Honest examination of what arose, including resistance and difficulty |
| Theoretical integration | Making sense of the creative process through therapeutic theory |
| Development over time | How your relationship to your own art-making has shifted |

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Making work specifically for the journal submission that is polished and performed rather than honest. Examiners are experienced art therapists — they can recognise the difference between authentic engagement and manufactured evidence.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Rhythm and Sustainability

The most common failure of a personal development log is gaps — weeks of not writing, followed by reconstruction. Reconstruction is not reflection. The value of the log depends on writing while the experience is still alive.

- Write within 24–48 hours of the experience you are reflecting on
- Keep entries brief enough to be sustainable — 250–400 words is a complete entry
- Even if the week was quiet — "nothing significant happened" — write about what that absence felt like

### ✍️ Boundaries and Ethics

- Never include identifying information about clients — not names, ages (unless they must be given generically), specific identifying details
- Apply the same standards to writing about colleagues and supervisors
- If in doubt about whether an entry is appropriate for a submitted document, discuss with your supervisor before including it

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Entries are dated and regular throughout the placement or training year
- [ ] Each entry addresses experience, emotional response, theory, practice implications, and supervision link
- [ ] Feelings are named specifically — not just "difficult" or "emotional"
- [ ] Theoretical connections include citations
- [ ] Reflexivity is present — your own assumptions and patterns are examined
- [ ] No identifying information about clients, colleagues, or supervisors
- [ ] A pattern synthesis is present — either as periodic summaries or a year-end review
- [ ] Development over time is visible — not all entries read the same
- [ ] For Art Therapy: visual journal is included, with artworks and written commentary
- [ ] For Art Therapy: artworks are dated and made throughout the year — not produced for submission
- [ ] All cited sources in APA 7th

---

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
The PDL is assessed alongside your placement portfolio and supervisor reports. These three documents should form a coherent picture of your clinical development. Significant discrepancies between what your log records and what your supervisor observes will be noticed — and discussed.

#### Diploma (e.g. Art Therapy)
The process journal is a formal assessed component, not supplementary evidence. Approach it with the same rigour as any written assignment. The dual nature of the log — visual and textual — is itself a reflection of the integrative character of art therapy as a discipline.
