What this file is

Every format here assesses the same intellectual content as its written equivalent. A presentation is not easier than an essay — it is differently shaped. The argument must still be present. The evidence must still be cited. The critical analysis must still happen. Only the structural container changes.

When to use alternative formats

Some programmes offer format choice as standard. Others require you to request an alternative as a reasonable adjustment. In either case, knowing the structure of each format in advance means you can make an informed choice — and prepare properly rather than improvising.

⚠️ Always confirm with your programme coordinator before submitting in an alternative format. Some modules have fixed formats; others offer structured choice. The structures here reflect standard academic conventions — your institution may specify variations.

Oral & spoken formats

Spoken formats assess the same analytical and argumentative competencies as written work. The challenge shifts from prose construction to verbal precision, pacing, and the ability to respond to questions in real time.

Oral format
Oral Presentation with Q&A
The most common alternative to an essay. You present your argument, evidence, and analysis verbally — typically with supporting slides — and then defend your position in a question-and-answer session. The Q&A is where the same depth of understanding required in an essay is tested live.
Argument Critical analysis Knowledge Communication
All degrees Typically 10–20 min + 5–10 min Q&A
  1. Opening hook (30–60 sec)A question, statistic, or brief scenario — orients the audience and signals why this matters
  2. Signpost your argument (1 min)State your thesis — the claim you will make — and how the presentation will build toward it
  3. Literature context (2–4 min)Summarise the field, not your full review — hit the key frameworks and the gap you're addressing
  4. Main argument sections (5–10 min)Each slide = one claim + evidence + analysis. Follow the same paragraph logic as a written essay.
  5. Counter-argument and rebuttal (1–2 min)Acknowledge the strongest opposing view and explain why your argument holds regardless
  6. Conclusion (1–2 min)Restate your thesis, synthesise your evidence, and state the wider implication clearly
  7. Q&A (5–10 min)Prepared answers for likely challenges; "that's an interesting point, let me address that by…" buys thinking time
Rehearse out loud, not just in your head. The gap between reading notes silently and speaking them fluently is much larger than it feels during preparation.
  1. One claim per slideThe slide title should be your argument, not a topic label. "Deficit models fail to account for structural barriers" is a title. "Disability models" is a heading.
  2. Maximum 5 lines of text per slideSlides are cues for your spoken argument — not a transcript. If you are reading your slides, you have too much text.
  3. Every image or figure needs a purposeVisuals should carry evidence or clarify a concept — not decorate. Label all figures and cite their source.
  4. References slide at the endFull APA references for every source cited. This is assessed even in oral formats.
  5. Speaker notes carry your depthWrite fuller notes in the speaker notes pane — these show your analytical depth if asked to submit slides as evidence of learning.
Never put everything you want to say on the slide — then read from it. Examiners mark your spoken argument. Slides that do all the work signal that you are not comfortable speaking to the material.

Oral format — asynchronous
Recorded Video Presentation
Structurally identical to a live presentation but recorded and submitted as a video file. The absence of real-time Q&A shifts weight toward the depth of your scripted argument. Many programmes now use this as a standard alternative — especially where accessibility or scheduling is a factor.
Argument Critical analysis Knowledge Communication
All degrees Typically 10–20 min + written reference list
  1. Follow the live presentation structure exactlyHook → thesis → literature context → argument sections → counter-argument → conclusion
  2. Write a full script or detailed outlineWithout the pressure of live delivery, there is no excuse for imprecision. Every claim should be deliberate.
  3. Include a written reference list as a submitted documentAPA 7th · submitted alongside the video file · assessed equally
  4. State your name, student number, and module at the startAcademic integrity — examiners need to verify authorship
  5. Closed captions if required by your institutionAuto-captions from your recording software + manual correction is the minimum standard
Record in multiple short takes by section rather than attempting one long take. Edit together — most institutions accept light editing. Practice each section until you can deliver it without reading verbatim.
  1. Audio quality matters more than video qualityA clear voice on a laptop camera is better than a blurry face with a USB microphone. Invert that: clear voice + decent lighting is the goal.
  2. Look at the camera, not your slidesEye contact with the lens signals command of your material. Glancing at slides constantly signals you are reading.
  3. Speak slower than feels naturalVideo playback makes fast speech harder to follow. Your natural pace on screen will feel slow to you — that is the target.
  4. Use screen-recording for slide-heavy contentTools: Loom, OBS, PowerPoint's built-in recorder, Zoom. Record slides + voiceover simultaneously.
  5. File format and sizeMP4 is universally accepted. Most institutions cap file size — compress using Handbrake if needed before submission.
Oral format — examined
Structured Oral Examination (Mini-Viva)
A formal oral examination in place of a written assignment — common in modules where depth of understanding matters as much as written fluency. You are given questions in advance (seen) or on the day (unseen) and examined in real time. The marker is directly probing the depth, accuracy, and critical quality of your knowledge.
Knowledge Critical analysis Argument Reflection
MSc MA PhD 20–45 min · 1–2 examiners
  1. Prepare as if writing the essay firstYour argument, evidence, and analysis should be completely worked out before the oral. The oral is the delivery mechanism — not the place to think through the content for the first time.
  2. Prepare a 2-minute summary of your core argumentOpening questions almost always invite you to state your position. Have this practiced and polished.
  3. Anticipate challenges to every claimFor each key point you want to make, ask: what is the strongest objection? Prepare a response.
  4. Know your sources deeplyYou may be asked to expand on any citation. "I'd have to check the paper" is not a sufficient answer for a cited source.
  5. Practise thinking aloudRecord yourself answering practice questions. The gap between thinking an answer and articulating it clearly is large — bridge it in preparation, not in the exam.
It is entirely acceptable to say "that's a nuanced point — I think the answer has two parts…" and then take a breath before answering. Composure under pressure is itself being assessed.
  1. "Can you summarise your main argument?"The opening — have a concise 90-second answer prepared. State thesis, key evidence, conclusion.
  2. "What is the strongest counter-argument to your position?"Tests depth and intellectual honesty. Name it precisely, then explain why your argument holds nonetheless.
  3. "Why did you choose [author/framework] rather than [alternative]?"Tests whether you have read critically or just cited what appeared first.
  4. "What are the limitations of your argument?"Every strong argument has acknowledged limits. Name them — then explain why the argument remains valid within those limits.
  5. "What would you read or do differently if you had more time?"Tests metacognition and genuine intellectual engagement with the topic.
Do not try to deflect a question you find difficult by pivoting to something you know better. Examiners notice this and it signals the gap in your knowledge more loudly than a careful partial answer would.

Visual & poster formats

Visual formats do not simplify content — they require you to distil complex argument into precise, economical form. The constraint of space forces clarity of thinking that extended prose can obscure.

Visual format
Academic Conference Poster
A single-page visual document presenting a research question, methodology, findings, and conclusion — structured for rapid comprehension. Common at research conferences and increasingly used as a module assessment. Often accompanied by a 2–3 minute verbal explanation (the "elevator pitch").
Knowledge Research Argument Communication
MSc MA PhD A0 or A1 · ~800 words total
  1. Title (visible from 3 metres)Your research question or a declarative claim — not a topic label. Large font. Your name and institution below.
  2. Introduction / Background (150 words max)Why does this question matter? What is the gap? State your research question explicitly.
  3. Methods (100–150 words + a visual)Design, participants/data, analysis approach. A simple diagram or table often communicates this faster than prose.
  4. Results / Findings (150–200 words + figures)1–3 key findings. Prioritise — do not try to present everything. Each finding should have a visual representation where possible.
  5. Discussion / Implications (150 words)What do the findings mean? How do they connect to existing literature? What is the contribution?
  6. Conclusion (50–80 words)One clear takeaway sentence + future directions. Should be readable as a standalone statement.
  7. References (10–15 key citations)Select only the most essential — APA 7th format · smaller font but fully legible
The poster should be readable in 3 minutes by someone who has never heard of your topic. If a colleague cannot summarise your argument after reading it in 3 minutes, it needs editing.
  1. Use a grid layoutTwo or three columns with consistent margins. Sections should align visually — misaligned blocks read as disorganised thinking.
  2. Hierarchy through size, not decorationTitle largest → section headings → body text → captions. Colour and bold should emphasise — not decorate.
  3. Every visual needs a caption and a sourceGraphs, photos, diagrams — all labelled. Unlabelled visuals fail on academic rigour criteria.
  4. White space is not wasted spaceDense posters are harder to read, not more impressive. Generous margins signal clarity of thought.
  5. Accessible colour choicesAvoid red/green combinations. Ensure sufficient contrast. Check readability in greyscale before printing.
Tools: Canva (free templates for A0/A1), PowerPoint (set custom slide size to A1 in cm), Adobe InDesign. Export as PDF at 300dpi for print.
Visual format
Scholarly Infographic
A visual argument combining text, data, and illustration to communicate a complex topic accessibly. Used most often in public health, social policy, and education modules. The challenge is maintaining academic rigour — full citations, evidenced claims — within a visual format designed for broad audiences.
Knowledge Argument Communication Critical analysis
MSc MA Diploma Often submitted with a 500-word rationale
  1. Headline claim (the title)A declarative statement of your argument — not a topic. "Social model of disability predicts better mental health outcomes" not "Disability and mental health".
  2. Context block2–3 sentences situating the issue. Who is affected, why it matters, what the current evidence shows.
  3. Visual evidence sections (3–5)Each section = one claim + a visual representation of its evidence (graph, icon set, quote, statistic). Arranged to flow logically — readers scan top-to-bottom or in a Z-pattern.
  4. Implication / call to actionWhat does this evidence mean? What should happen? Connects back to your headline claim.
  5. Sources sectionSmall-font APA references for every statistic, quote, and figure used. Non-negotiable for academic submission.
  1. Every statistic needs a citationIf you cannot cite the source in the infographic, do not use the statistic. This is where infographics most commonly fail academically.
  2. Submit a written rationale alongsideMost academic infographic assessments require a 300–800 word rationale explaining: your argument, your design choices, your audience, and your evidence selection.
  3. Acknowledge limitationsThe infographic simplifies — your rationale document is the place to acknowledge what complexity was omitted and why.
  4. Do not misrepresent data visuallyTruncated axes, disproportionate icon sets, and cherry-picked time periods are academic integrity violations — not just design errors.
The rationale document is often where the critical analysis criteria are most fully demonstrated. Treat it as seriously as the visual itself.
Visual format
Annotated Concept Map
A visual representation of the relationships between ideas, theories, or concepts — with annotations explaining each connection. More rigorous than a mind map; closer to a visual literature review or theoretical framework. Highly effective for demonstrating understanding of complex, interconnected fields.
Knowledge Critical analysis Argument
All degrees Submitted with a written commentary (500–1,000 words)
  1. Central conceptYour core research question or the main theoretical concept — placed at the centre or top of the map.
  2. First-order nodesThe 3–5 major themes, theories, or frameworks that directly relate to the central concept. Each node = a labelled box or circle.
  3. Second-order nodesSubconcepts, specific authors, empirical findings, or examples that branch from each major theme.
  4. Labelled connectionsThe lines between nodes carry the intellectual weight — each line should be labelled with the nature of the relationship (e.g. "challenges", "extends", "is mediated by", "contradicts").
  5. Written commentaryExplains 3–5 of the most significant or non-obvious connections in the map — this is where your critical analysis lives.
An unlabelled concept map is just a picture. The labels on connections are what demonstrate critical understanding — they are what separates this from a brainstorm.
  1. Use different connection types visuallySolid line = direct relationship · dashed = contested or provisional · arrow direction = causal relationship · double-headed = bidirectional influence
  2. Include author citations at nodesName the theorist or study your node represents — "(Bronfenbrenner, 1979)" next to the ecological model node — this shows your map is grounded in literature, not intuition.
  3. Show tension as well as connectionA truly sophisticated concept map shows where frameworks contradict or compete — not just where they agree. Mark these connections clearly.
Tools: Miro (free tier), Lucidchart, Obsidian (with Canvas plugin), or drawn by hand and photographed. The medium is less important than the intellectual content.

Digital & multimedia formats

Digital formats increasingly appear in programmes that emphasise professional communication skills alongside academic rigour. Each format has its own structural conventions — and its own distinctive failure modes.

Digital format — audio
Podcast / Audio Essay
A scripted, recorded audio piece presenting an academic argument for a non-specialist audience. Used in public health, social science, and education programmes to assess communication of complex ideas accessibly. The structure mirrors an essay — but the register shifts, and your citational practice adapts to the medium.
Argument Knowledge Communication Critical analysis
MSc MA Diploma 10–20 min · submitted with full script + references
  1. Cold open (30 sec)Start mid-thought — a striking question, a brief scenario, a counterintuitive statistic. Do not open with "Hello and welcome to…"
  2. Introduce the question and your argument (1–2 min)State what you are going to argue — clearly and in plain language. Listeners cannot re-read.
  3. Contextual section (2–4 min)What does the audience need to know to follow your argument? Keep to what is essential — this is not a literature review, it is orientation.
  4. Main argument sections (6–10 min)3–4 clearly signposted claims, each with evidence and analysis. Verbal signposting ("my second point is…") is essential because listeners cannot see your structure.
  5. Conclusion (1–2 min)Restate your argument and its significance. End memorably — return to your opening image, answer your opening question, or leave the listener with a thought to sit with.
  6. Submit full script + reference listEvery claim that would require a citation in an essay must be cited in your submitted script. The audio itself adapts ("researchers at UCL found…") but the script must carry full APA citations.
  1. Write for the ear, not the eyeShort sentences. Active voice. Concrete language. Complex subordinate clauses that read well are often impossible to follow aurally.
  2. Signpost constantlyIn writing, structure is visible. In audio, it must be stated. "That brings me to my main argument…" / "To summarise what I've argued so far…" are not redundant — they are essential.
  3. Cite without disrupting flow"Research by Hannah Frith and colleagues" rather than "(Frith et al., 2021)". Full APA citation goes in your script — spoken attribution suffices in the recording.
  4. Read your script aloud before recordingSentences that look fine on the page often stumble in speech. Every sentence should feel natural to say — rewrite anything that makes you hesitate.
Record audio in a small, soft-furnished room (a wardrobe full of clothes is a surprisingly effective recording booth). Avoid rooms with hard floors and bare walls — the echo will dominate.
Digital format — web
Digital Portfolio or Scholarly Website
A curated collection of work, reflection, and evidence presented through a digital platform. Common in art therapy, counselling, and education programmes — it allows you to demonstrate development over time rather than a single snapshot. The academic challenge is that curation itself is an intellectual act requiring a clear argument about what the evidence shows.
Reflection Argument Knowledge Communication
MA Diploma PhD Ongoing curation + written introductory statement
  1. Introductory statement (500–800 words)Your argument about what this portfolio demonstrates. Not "here is my work" — but "this portfolio shows the development of my thinking about X, as evidenced by Y."
  2. Thematic or chronological sectionsOrganise by theme (clinical practice / reflective development / theoretical engagement) or chronologically (Year 1 / placement / Year 2). Choose one structure and apply it consistently.
  3. Each artefact has a brief reflective commentary (100–200 words)Why is this piece included? What does it demonstrate? What does it show about your learning at that point?
  4. Evidence of developmentThe most compelling portfolios show change — include early work alongside later work and name the difference explicitly.
  5. Concluding reflectionWhat does the portfolio as a whole demonstrate about your learning? Where are you now, and where are you going?
  1. More is not betterA curated portfolio of 8 carefully chosen and well-annotated pieces is stronger than 30 pieces with no commentary. Selection is itself an intellectual act.
  2. Include work that challenged you, not just your best workPortfolios that only show success are unconvincing. Examiners understand that learning involves difficulty — including a piece that went wrong, with honest reflection, shows maturity.
  3. Ethical permissions for any client materialIf any piece involves client work, case material, or placement documentation — verify your institution's permissions requirements before including it, even anonymised.
Platforms: WordPress, Notion, GitHub Pages, Wix. Check whether your institution requires a private (password-protected) submission or permits a public portfolio — this affects both platform choice and what you can include.
Digital format — textual
Annotated Document with Critical Commentary
A source document (a policy, a clinical guideline, a research paper, a legal text) annotated throughout with critical analysis — then accompanied by a structured commentary that synthesises your critique. Effectively externalises the analytical process that normally happens invisibly inside an essay.
Critical analysis Knowledge Argument Research
MSc MA PhD Annotated document + 1,000–2,000 word commentary
  1. Source selection and rationale (200 words)Why this document? What makes it significant, contested, or representative of a key debate in your field?
  2. Annotations throughout the documentMargin comments or footnotes at key passages: identifying assumptions, noting what is absent, linking claims to supporting or contradicting literature, questioning the evidence base.
  3. Synthesis commentary (1,000–1,500 words)What does your annotation reveal about the document as a whole? What are its central limitations? What does it assume? Where does it succeed?
  4. Implications sectionWhat should a practitioner, policymaker, or researcher take from your critique? What would a better version of this document look like?
  5. Reference listEvery source cited in your annotations and commentary — APA 7th
This format is particularly well-suited to Disability Studies (critiquing policy documents) and Counselling Psychology (critiquing clinical guidelines). It makes your analytical process visible in a way that an essay cannot.

Creative & narrative formats

These formats are most common in fields where narrative and lived experience are treated as valid epistemological sources — Disability Studies, Counselling Psychology, Art Therapy. The critical error is treating them as easier than conventional academic formats. They are not. They require the same intellectual rigour with an additional layer of craft.

Creative format — hybrid
Creative-Critical Piece with Exegesis
A piece of creative writing (narrative, poetry, autoethnography, fictional case vignette) that embodies your argument — followed by a critical exegesis that explains how the creative piece works and what theoretical claims it makes. The two parts are assessed together. The creative element is not decorative; it carries part of the intellectual argument.
Argument Reflection Knowledge Critical analysis
MSc MA Diploma Creative piece (500–1,500 words) + exegesis (1,000–2,000 words)
  1. Creative pieceWritten as its own self-contained work. Should be able to stand alone — but is deepened by the exegesis. The form (poetry, narrative, dialogue, vignette) should be chosen because it uniquely serves the argument.
  2. Exegesis — theoretical framingWhat theoretical framework or literature does the creative piece draw on? How does the form itself enact or embody a theoretical claim?
  3. Exegesis — craft decisionsExplain your formal choices: why this voice, this structure, this imagery? Each choice should have a reasoned justification grounded in your argument.
  4. Exegesis — limitations and reflexivityWhat does this creative approach allow that conventional academic writing cannot? What does it foreclose or risk? How does your own positionality shape the work?
  5. Reference listCite both your theoretical sources and any creative influences you drew on consciously.
The exegesis is not a translation of the creative piece into academic language. It is an analytical account of how and why the creative piece was made — and what claims it makes that the creative form allows uniquely.
  1. Frame the creative piece theoretically (300–400 words)What body of literature does this piece sit within? Autoethnography? Narrative inquiry? Poetic inquiry? Name the tradition and situate yourself within it.
  2. Account for your formal choices (400–600 words)Why this form? Why first person or third? Why fragmented or continuous? Each choice should be defensible in relation to your argument.
  3. Connect to your academic argument explicitly (400–600 words)What claim does the creative piece make? How does it make that claim in ways that conventional academic writing cannot? Be precise.
  4. Reflexive positioning (200–300 words)How does your own experience, identity, or positionality relate to the material? This is not optional — it is part of what makes creative-critical work academically honest.
Creative-clinical format
Fictional Case Vignette with Clinical Analysis
A constructed client scenario written to illustrate a clinical concept or dilemma — followed by a structured clinical analysis applying your theoretical framework. Used in Counselling and Clinical Psychology to assess clinical reasoning without requiring real client data. The "fictional" element must be clinically realistic and theoretically purposeful, not imaginative.
Knowledge Critical analysis Reflection Argument
MA Diploma PhD Vignette (300–500 words) + analysis (1,500–3,000 words)
  1. The vignette — construct the client scenarioPresenting concern · brief developmental and social history · what brings the client now · relevant cultural and contextual factors. Written in narrative form — should feel clinically plausible.
  2. Theoretical formulationApply your chosen framework to the case. How does this theory explain the presentation? What does it highlight and what does it miss?
  3. Treatment approach and rationaleWhat intervention would you propose? Why this approach for this client? What does the evidence say?
  4. Ethical and relational considerationsWhat ethical issues does this case raise? Power dynamics, cultural considerations, boundaries, risk.
  5. Reflexive accountWhat does this case evoke in you? How might your own background affect the therapeutic relationship? What would you bring to supervision?
The vignette must be clinically realistic — not a vehicle to showcase theory. Write the client first, then apply the framework. A vignette that exists only to illustrate a concept reads as unconvincing and is marked as such.
Creative format — autoethnographic
Autoethnographic Essay
A rigorous form of qualitative inquiry that uses personal experience as data to illuminate cultural, social, or theoretical phenomena. Not memoir — it is systematic, theoretically grounded, and reflexive. Particularly relevant in Disability Studies and Counselling Psychology where researcher positionality is central to the methodology.
Reflection Critical analysis Knowledge Argument
MSc MA Diploma PhD 2,000–5,000 words
  1. Theoretical positioningName the autoethnographic tradition you are working within (evocative, analytical, critical). Justify why this methodology serves your question.
  2. Personal narrativeWritten in first person, past or present tense. Specific, sensory, scene-based — not abstract summary. This is the empirical material.
  3. Analytical movesInterrupt the narrative with analytical passages that connect your experience to theoretical concepts, broader social patterns, or existing research.
  4. Reflexive examinationWhat are the limits of using your own experience as data? Who might read this differently? What do you risk in writing it?
  5. Contribution statementWhat does this autoethnographic account illuminate about the social phenomenon you are examining — beyond your individual story?
  1. Substantive contribution, not personal expressionEvery narrative passage must be serving an analytical purpose. "This happened to me" is not sufficient — "this happened to me and it illuminates X social phenomenon in the following way" is the standard.
  2. Literature throughout, not only in theory sectionsCite relevant research and theory within the narrative passages where they illuminate what you are describing — not only in separate analytical sections.
  3. Name your methodologyWhich scholars theorise the form of autoethnography you are using? (Ellis, Adams, Bochner; Holman Jones; Chang) — your methodology section should be as rigorous as in a conventional qualitative study.
The key question an examiner asks of any autoethnographic piece: "Does this illuminate something beyond the individual?" If the answer is yes and the connection is made explicit — the piece is academically sound.

Group & collaborative formats

Group assessments are less common at postgraduate level but appear in some programmes — particularly where professional collaboration is a core competency. Individual contribution is usually assessed alongside the group product.

Collaborative format
Group Presentation with Individual Contribution Statement
A collaborative oral presentation on a shared topic, where each member delivers a distinct section — assessed both on the group product and individual contribution. The intellectual challenge is coherence: each individual section must be analytically sound and connect to the others to form a unified argument, not just adjacent independent talks.
Argument Knowledge Critical analysis Communication
MSc MA Diploma 20–40 min group + individual 400–600 word statement
  1. Shared opening — one person introduces the group's thesisEstablish the overall argument the group will make. Not just a topic — a claim. Each individual section should build toward this.
  2. Individual sections (5–10 min each)Each section = one coherent analytical argument. Open with your section's claim, build with evidence and analysis, conclude by connecting back to the group's thesis.
  3. Explicit transitions between speakers"Aiko has shown that X — my section builds on that by examining Y." Transitions demonstrate integration, not just handoff.
  4. Shared conclusion — one person synthesisesDraw together the threads from each section. Restate the group's thesis and what you have collectively demonstrated.
  5. Q&A — all members respondAny member may be asked any question — you are all responsible for understanding the whole presentation, not just your section.
The most common failure is five disconnected talks sharing a title slide. If each section could be delivered in isolation without any connection to the others, the group has not done the intellectual work of integration.
  1. What you contributed (specific)Not "I researched my section" — but specifically: the argument you developed, the literature you brought in, the editorial decisions you made about the group's framing.
  2. How your contribution connected to the group's thesisDemonstrate that you understand how your section serves the larger argument — not just that you completed your part.
  3. Critical reflection on the collaborative processWhat was intellectually productive about working with others on this? What would you do differently? This is not a complaint section — it is a reflective one.
Keep a brief log during the preparation process — dates of meetings, what was decided, what you drafted. This protects you if the group's contribution is uneven and you need to evidence your own work.
Collaborative format — debate
Academic Symposium / Structured Debate
A moderated academic discussion in which participants present and defend positions on a contested question. You may argue a position you do not personally hold — the assessment is on the quality of your argumentation, use of evidence, and ability to engage with opposing positions, not on your conclusion.
Argument Critical analysis Knowledge Communication
MSc MA PhD Usually 60–90 min · followed by individual reflection (500 words)
  1. Know your position deeplyResearch the strongest arguments for your assigned position — including sources you might personally disagree with. The quality of your argument depends on the quality of your preparation, not your prior views.
  2. Anticipate and prepare for counter-argumentsIdentify the 3 strongest objections to your position and prepare responses to each. In debate, you will face these in real time.
  3. Opening statement (3–5 min)State your position · your key argument · the evidence base you will draw on · why opposing positions are insufficient
  4. Rebuttal (live)Listen carefully to opposing arguments. Identify the specific claim you are rebutting — "you argued X, but Y evidence suggests otherwise" is stronger than a general counter-point.
  5. Individual reflection (post-debate)What did you learn from defending a position, regardless of your personal views? What arguments surprised you? What does this reveal about the complexity of the question?
Being assigned a position you disagree with is academically valuable — it forces you to understand an opposing view at depth. The best debaters can argue both sides convincingly because they understand both sides genuinely.
