# 🔭 Research Proposal

> **A complete reference for taught programme and PhD application proposals**
> A research proposal is a promise: that you have a viable question, a credible plan for answering it, and the intellectual preparation to carry it out. It is assessed on the quality of your thinking, not the certainty of your outcomes.

## 1. Title & Rationale

> **Typical length:** 10–15% of total word count
> *(Taught proposal: ~150–300 words · PhD application: ~500–800 words)*

The rationale answers three questions: why this topic, why now, and why you. It should move from broad significance to specific problem to your proposed study, without excessive background.

---

### 1.1 Title

Your working title should be specific enough to signal your focus but flexible enough to survive contact with the research process. A strong research proposal title often contains:

- The core phenomenon or concept you are studying
- The population or context
- The methodological angle (optional at proposal stage, but can add precision)

> 📝 **Tip:** Write a working title first, then revise it at the end of the proposal when you know exactly what you are proposing. A vague title ("An investigation into mental health") signals an unresolved question.

---

### 1.2 Rationale

- Open by establishing the significance of the broader field or problem
- Narrow to the specific gap or underexplored question your study addresses
- Be direct about why this matters — practically, theoretically, or for the people affected
- Brief personal positioning is appropriate, especially at PhD level: why are *you* the right person to ask this question?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Rationales that claim sweeping significance without evidence ("mental health is the defining crisis of our time") or that are purely personal without scholarly grounding. Ground significance in the literature.

---

## 2. Research Question

> **The single most important sentence in the proposal**

Everything else in the proposal exists to show that this question is worth asking and that you can answer it. The question must be specific, answerable with the proposed methods, and scoped to the available time and resources.

---

### 2.1 Characteristics of a Strong Research Question

| Characteristic | What it means | Example of the problem |
|---------------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Specific** | Names the phenomenon, population, and context precisely | "Mental health" is not specific; "depression in autistic adults in supported employment" is |
| **Answerable** | Can be investigated with your proposed methods | A question about "the meaning of justice" cannot be answered with a survey |
| **Scoped** | Realistic for the degree level and time available | A PhD question at MSc scale will not go deep enough; an MSc question at PhD scale cannot be answered in time |
| **Significant** | Matters beyond your own curiosity | The answer should contribute something to the field, practice, or policy |
| **Open** | Cannot be answered yes or no | Binary questions foreclose the inquiry before it begins |

> 💡 **Key question:** What, specifically, will you know at the end of your study that you do not know now? If you cannot answer this, your question needs more work.

---

### 2.2 Sub-questions (if applicable)

One primary research question is standard. Sub-questions are appropriate when the main question has distinct components that each require separate investigation — but they should not simply restate the main question at a more granular level.

## 3. Literature Context & Gap

> **Typical length:** 25–35% of total word count
> *This section is a condensed critical literature review — not a comprehensive one*

Demonstrate that you know the field well enough to identify precisely where your study fits within it. At proposal stage, reviewers are not expecting a complete literature review — they are expecting evidence that you can read critically and locate a genuine gap.

---

### 3.1 What the Field Currently Knows

- Identify the two to four most significant bodies of literature relevant to your question
- Synthesise what they establish — do not summarise study by study
- Note areas of consensus and areas of ongoing debate
- Be selective: cover what is necessary to understand the gap, not everything that exists

---

### 3.2 The Gap

- Name the gap precisely: what has not been studied, by whom, in what context, using what methods, through what theoretical lens?
- Explain why the gap exists — is it a new phenomenon, a methodological limitation, a theoretical blind spot, or an underserved population?
- Show why filling the gap matters — for theory, practice, policy, or the people affected

> 💡 **Key question:** Could someone argue that your gap is not actually a gap — that it has already been studied? If so, you need to know those studies and explain why your study is still needed.

---

## 4. Methodology

> **Typical length:** 25–30% of total word count

The methodology section demonstrates that your proposed approach is appropriate to your question and that you understand how to use it. It is not just a description of what you will do — it is a justification of *why* these choices.

---

### 4.1 Research Design

State your overall approach and specific design type clearly.

| Design type | Examples | When appropriate |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Qualitative** | Thematic analysis, phenomenology, grounded theory, discourse analysis | Questions about experience, meaning, process, or context |
| **Quantitative** | Survey, longitudinal cohort, RCT, quasi-experimental | Questions about prevalence, effect, prediction, or causal relationship |
| **Mixed methods** | Sequential explanatory, concurrent triangulation | When neither approach alone can answer the question |
| **Practice-based / arts-informed** | A/r/tography, visual inquiry, autoethnography | Questions about creative practice, embodied knowledge, or practitioner experience |

Also state your epistemological position — interpretivist, constructivist, post-positivist, critical realist — and explain how it aligns with your design.

> 📝 **Tip:** In Disability Studies and Counselling Psychology, the epistemological section often carries as much weight as the method itself. Do not treat it as a formality.

---

### 4.2 Participants or Data Sources

- Who will participate, or what data will you analyse?
- How will participants be recruited or data accessed?
- What are your inclusion and exclusion criteria?
- How many participants or data sources, and how did you determine this?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Stating a sample size without justification. In qualitative research, justify saturation; in quantitative research, show a power calculation or reference comparable studies.

---

### 4.3 Data Collection and Analysis

- What instruments or procedures will you use to collect data? (Interview schedule, observation protocol, validated scale, archival search)
- How will you analyse the data? Name the specific approach — not just "thematic analysis" but "Braun and Clarke's (2006) reflexive thematic analysis"
- How will you ensure rigour? (Credibility, transferability, dependability in qualitative; reliability and validity in quantitative)

---

### 4.4 Limitations

Every methodology has limitations — naming them demonstrates sophistication, not weakness.

- What will your design *not* be able to tell you?
- What are the known limitations of your chosen method?
- How will you mitigate the most significant ones?

---

## 5. Ethical Considerations

> **Typical length:** 8–12% of total word count

Ethical consideration is not a bureaucratic checklist — it is a demonstration that you have thought carefully about the impact of your research on the people involved in it and on the field.

---

### 5.1 Core Considerations

- **Informed consent:** How will participants be informed about the study, and how will consent be obtained and documented?
- **Confidentiality and anonymisation:** How will you protect participant identity in data storage, analysis, and reporting?
- **Right to withdraw:** How will participants be able to withdraw, and what happens to their data if they do?
- **Risk of harm:** Are there risks to participants — psychological, social, legal, physical — and how will you mitigate them?
- **Power dynamics:** Are there power differentials between you and participants that might affect voluntary participation?

---

### 5.2 Positionality

At postgraduate level in social science and health fields, ethical consideration includes your own positionality — how your background, identity, and relationship to the topic might shape the research.

- What is your relationship to the topic or population?
- What assumptions might you bring that could affect data collection or analysis?
- How will you remain reflexive throughout the process?

> 📝 **Tip:** Ethics applications follow after the proposal — but the proposal should demonstrate that you have thought through the ethical terrain thoroughly enough that the application will be straightforward.

---

## 6. Timeline

> **Typically presented as a table or Gantt chart**

A realistic, phased timeline demonstrates that you understand what the research involves and that your proposal is deliverable within the degree period.

---

### 6.1 Typical Phases

| Phase | What it includes |
|-------|----------------|
| **Preparation** | Ethics application, finalising instruments, participant recruitment |
| **Data collection** | Fieldwork, interviews, surveys, archival research |
| **Analysis** | Coding, thematic development, statistical analysis |
| **Writing** | Chapter drafting, supervisor review cycles, revision |
| **Completion** | Final editing, formatting, submission |

- Be honest about how long each phase takes — reviewers know what is realistic
- Build in buffer time; research rarely proceeds on schedule
- For PhD proposals, the timeline spans 3–4 years; for taught programmes, it spans the degree duration

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Timelines that allocate most time to data collection and almost none to analysis and writing. Analysis and writing together typically take as long as data collection, often longer.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Taught Programme vs PhD Application

| Feature | Taught proposal (MSc/MA/Dip) | PhD application proposal |
|---------|----------------------------|------------------------|
| Word count | 1,000–3,000 words | 5,000–10,000 words |
| Literature depth | Demonstrating familiarity | Demonstrating command |
| Methodology | Outline-level | Detailed and justified |
| Timeline | Semester-level | Year-level across 3–4 years |
| Supervisor matching | Usually assigned | You are proposing to a specific supervisor |
| Originality | Applying existing methods | Original contribution expected |

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Title is specific and reflects the actual study proposed
- [ ] Rationale establishes significance grounded in the literature
- [ ] Research question is specific, answerable, scoped, and open
- [ ] Literature context demonstrates critical familiarity with the field
- [ ] Gap is precisely identified and its significance is explained
- [ ] Research design is named and justified as appropriate to the question
- [ ] Epistemological position is stated and consistent with the design
- [ ] Participants or data sources are specified with recruitment plan
- [ ] Data collection instruments or procedures are described
- [ ] Analysis approach is named specifically — not just a general category
- [ ] Limitations are acknowledged
- [ ] Ethical considerations are addressed (consent, risk, positionality)
- [ ] Timeline is realistic and covers all phases including writing
- [ ] All sources cited correctly in APA 7th

---

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
The taught proposal is often the foundation of your dissertation. A strong proposal at this stage saves significant time later — treat it as the first draft of your methodology chapter.

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
Ethics is especially central given the clinical population you may be studying. Your positionality as a practitioner-researcher — and how it might affect data collection — deserves particular attention.

#### Diploma (e.g. Art Therapy)
Practice-based and arts-informed methodologies are legitimate — name them explicitly and cite the methodological literature that supports them (Sullivan, Leavy, McNiff).

#### PhD
The PhD proposal is effectively a job application. Supervisors are asking: can this person think independently about a scholarly problem? Show that you understand the field's existing debates — not just the topic.
