# 📝 Written Examination

> **A complete reference for postgraduate written exams**
> A postgraduate written exam rewards the same qualities as coursework: a clear argument, critical engagement with theory, and evidenced claims. The difference is that you produce those qualities under time pressure, without your notes. Preparation is the entire game.

## 1. Types of Postgraduate Exam

---

### 1.1 Unseen Exam

Questions are not released in advance. You know the general topic areas but not the specific questions.

- More common at undergraduate level but appear in some postgraduate programmes
- Preparation focuses on breadth of knowledge and flexible argument formation
- You must be able to construct a clear position on a question you have not seen before

---

### 1.2 Seen Exam (Released Questions)

Questions are released in advance — sometimes days, sometimes weeks — but you write answers under timed conditions without notes.

- Common at postgraduate level because it rewards deeper engagement over memorisation
- Preparation focuses on *understanding* the questions, not *memorising* an answer
- You can plan your argument in advance, but must be able to reproduce the reasoning without notes

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Memorising a fixed answer to a seen question and reproducing it verbatim. Examiners can tell the difference between a memorised answer and a constructed argument. Memorised answers also collapse when a question is worded slightly differently than you expected.

---

### 1.3 Short Answer vs Essay Questions

| Format | What it requires |
|--------|----------------|
| **Essay questions** (1,000–1,500 words per question in the time available) | A structured argument with claim, evidence, analysis, counter-argument, conclusion |
| **Short answer questions** (200–400 words) | Precise, accurate, concise — demonstrate knowledge without extended argument |
| **Clinical vignette questions** | Apply theory to a presented case; demonstrate clinical reasoning |

This guide focuses primarily on essay-format questions, which are the most common in social science and psychology programmes.

## 2. Preparation

> **The exam is won in preparation, not in the exam room**

---

### 2.1 Know the Format Completely

Before beginning any content preparation, confirm:

- How many questions? How many must you answer?
- How long is the exam? How does that divide across questions?
- Is it seen or unseen?
- Are notes or materials permitted? (Rare at postgraduate level, but some open-book formats exist)
- What are the marking criteria?

---

### 2.2 What to Prepare

For each likely topic area:

| Prepare | Why |
|---------|-----|
| **A clear position on the key debates** | You need to be able to construct an argument, not just describe the field |
| **The strongest counter-arguments** | Examiners expect you to engage with opposition |
| **Five to eight key sources per topic** | Author, date, key finding, and one line of critical evaluation per source |
| **Two to three illustrative examples** | Concrete examples anchor abstract arguments under pressure |
| **Definitions of central concepts** | Precision with terminology matters; have these prepared |

> 📝 **Tip:** Do not try to prepare everything. Identify the five to six most likely topic areas and prepare deeply for each. Shallow preparation across twenty topics will not produce strong essays.

---

### 2.3 Preparing Arguments, Not Facts

The most common exam preparation mistake is trying to memorise content rather than preparing arguments. Facts remembered in isolation collapse under exam pressure. Arguments remembered as logical structures hold.

For each topic, prepare a skeleton argument:

1. **My position is:** [one sentence]
2. **This is supported by:** [three main claims, each with one key source]
3. **The strongest objection is:** [one sentence]
4. **My response to the objection is:** [one sentence]
5. **The broader implication is:** [one sentence]

Practice moving through this skeleton in your head. Under exam conditions, you can reconstruct the argument from the skeleton even if specific details escape you.

---

### 2.4 For Seen Exams

When you receive the questions:

1. **Read all questions carefully** before planning any answer
2. **Identify what each question is actually asking** — the topic is rarely the question; look for the analytical directive (compare, evaluate, critically examine, to what extent)
3. **Plan your argument** for each question you might answer — not a memorised essay, but a clear position with three main claims
4. **Identify your strongest question** — the one where you have the most precise knowledge and the clearest position
5. **Live with the questions** for a few days before the exam; let your thinking develop

## 3. In the Exam Room

---

### 3.1 Time Management

This is the single most important practical skill in a written exam.

| Exam duration | Questions | Time allocation |
|--------------|-----------|----------------|
| 2 hours | 2 essays | 55 min per essay + 10 min planning total |
| 3 hours | 3 essays | 55 min per essay + 15 min planning total |
| 2 hours | 1 essay + 4 short answers | 70 min essay + ~20 min per short answer |

**The non-negotiable rule:** A partial answer to all questions *always* outperforms a perfect answer to one. Markers cannot award marks for essays not written. If you run over time on question one, stop and move on.

---

### 3.2 Question Selection (for choice exams)

If you have a choice of questions:

1. Read all questions before choosing
2. Do not choose based on the topic alone — choose based on where you have the clearest argument and the most precise evidence
3. Avoid questions that seem appealing because you "know a lot about" the topic — knowledge without a clear argument produces description, not argument

---

### 3.3 Planning Before Writing

Spend 8–10 minutes planning each essay before writing a word of it. Under exam pressure, the temptation is to start writing immediately — resist this.

A 5-line plan:
1. My argument / thesis statement
2. Main point 1 (with key source)
3. Main point 2 (with key source)
4. Main point 3 or counter-argument (with key source)
5. Conclusion: what this argument shows

Writing from a plan produces a coherent essay. Writing without a plan produces a discovery essay — which is useful in drafting but not in an exam.

## 4. Writing Under Time Pressure

---

### 4.1 The Exam Essay Structure

The same as a coursework essay, compressed:

| Section | What to include | Approximate time (55 min essay) |
|---------|----------------|--------------------------------|
| **Introduction** | Context (1–2 sentences) + thesis statement | 5 min |
| **Body** | 3 paragraphs, each: claim + evidence + analysis | 35 min |
| **Counter-argument** | Named, explained, and rebutted | 8 min |
| **Conclusion** | Thesis restated + synthesis + implication | 7 min |

---

### 4.2 Writing Without Notes

When a citation is not perfectly recalled:

- Use author and approximate date: "Smith (around 2015) argues..."
- Describe the study accurately even without exact statistics: "In a large longitudinal study of..."
- Prioritise accuracy of argument over precision of citation

> 📝 **Tip:** Examiners know you are writing from memory. A slightly imprecise citation that shows genuine engagement with the material is more impressive than an absent one.

---

### 4.3 If You Run Dry Mid-Essay

If your thinking runs out before the word count does:

- Return to your plan and check which point you are making
- Ask: what is the counter-argument to what I have just said?
- Ask: what are the practical or theoretical implications of this point?
- Never pad — an essay that stops when the argument is complete is better than one padded to fill time

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Before the Exam

| Timing | Action |
|--------|--------|
| 3–4 weeks before | Identify topic areas; prepare skeleton arguments and key sources |
| 2 weeks before | Practise writing essay plans (10 min each) for likely questions |
| 1 week before | Practise writing timed essays — full length, under real conditions |
| Night before | Review skeleton arguments only — rest, do not cram |
| Morning of | Do not start new material; review only what you know |

### ✍️ Practise Under Real Conditions

The most effective exam preparation is writing under real conditions — timed, without notes. One timed practice essay is worth ten hours of reading. Find past exam questions or construct your own from the syllabus, and write full responses to time.

---

### ✅ Submission / End-of-Exam Checklist

- [ ] All required questions answered (do not leave one blank)
- [ ] Each essay has a clear thesis statement in the introduction
- [ ] Each body paragraph has a topic sentence, evidence, and analysis
- [ ] A counter-argument is engaged with in each essay
- [ ] Each essay has a conclusion that synthesises rather than repeats
- [ ] Time was divided approximately equally across all questions
- [ ] Citations are present, even if approximate
- [ ] No essay is left unfinished — a partial essay always beats no essay

---

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
Exams at MSc level in social science programmes often focus on theoretical debates. The examiner is asking whether you can take a position in a contested field and defend it with evidence — not whether you can reproduce lecture notes.

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
Some MA programmes include clinical vignette questions in examinations — presenting a brief client scenario and asking you to formulate and plan an intervention. The case formulation structure ([Case Formulation guide](CASE_FORMULATION_CLINICAL.md)) applies directly, compressed to exam conditions.
