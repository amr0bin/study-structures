# 📚 Thesis Structure & Writing Guide

> **A complete reference for MSc, MA, and PhD theses**
> This guide decodes thesis structure so your mental energy stays where it belongs — on your original thinking, your data, and your argument. The structure holds across MSc and PhD; what scales is the depth, word count, and originality expected of you.

## 1. Introduction

> **Typical length:** 5–10% of total word count
> *(MSc: ~1,000–1,500 words · PhD: ~3,000–5,000 words)*

The introduction is your **contract with the reader**. By the end of it they should know exactly what problem you are studying, why it matters, and what specific question your thesis will answer. It is also the section most examiners read first *and* last — so it needs to be tight, confident, and precise.

---

### 1.1 Rationale

This is your opening argument for why this research needs to exist. Start broad — establish the real-world or theoretical context — then funnel down to the specific problem you are addressing. Think of it as the answer to: *So what?*

- Open with a compelling observation, statistic, or tension in the field
- Establish the significance: who is affected, what is at stake, why now?
- Avoid inflated claims — *"this is the most important issue of our time"* signals weak argumentation
- End this subsection pointing clearly toward the gap your study addresses

> 💡 **Key question:** If a non-specialist asked *"why does this matter?"*, could you answer in two sentences?

---

### 1.2 Structure Overview

A brief roadmap of what the reader will encounter in each chapter. This is functional, not decorative — it helps examiners navigate a long document and signals that your logic is ordered.

- One to two sentences per chapter is sufficient
- Write this **last**, after you know what each chapter actually contains
- Use consistent, precise language that mirrors your actual chapter headings

> 📝 **Tip:** Write a placeholder here early, then revise it once your full draft is complete. Many students find the structure shifts during writing.

---

### 1.3 Research Question

The most important sentence in your thesis. Everything you write, in every section, should be in service of this question. It must be specific, answerable with the methods you are using, and appropriately scoped.

- One primary research question is standard; secondary sub-questions are acceptable if needed
- A good research question **cannot** be answered with a simple yes or no
- It should not be so broad that a literature review would answer it, or so narrow that it becomes trivial
- If you have hypotheses (quantitative work), state them here

> 💡 **Key question:** Can you identify which section of your thesis answers this question? That section is your Results or Findings — everything else supports it.

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** The most common introduction mistake is burying the research question at the end of a long literature survey. State it clearly and early — by the end of section 1.3.

## 2. Literature Review

> **Typical length:** 20–30% of total word count
> *(MSc: ~4,000–6,000 words · PhD: ~12,000–20,000 words)*

The literature review is **not** a summary of everything you have read. It is a structured critical argument that maps existing knowledge, traces its development, evaluates its strengths and weaknesses, and locates precisely where your study sits within — and contributes to — that body of knowledge.

---

### 2.1 Theoretical Framework

Identify the theoretical lens or lenses through which you are examining your topic. This is the conceptual architecture that shapes what questions you ask, what you consider relevant data, and how you interpret what you find.

- Name and explain the theory or theories you are working within
- Justify why this framework is appropriate for *your question* — not just because it is popular
- In social science, disability studies, counselling, and clinical psych, this section is often substantial and deeply contested — engage with that critically
- If using multiple frameworks (e.g. intersectionality + critical realism), explain how they relate to each other

> 📝 **Tip:** Your theoretical framework will determine your methodology. A reader should be able to predict your methods from reading this section. If there is a mismatch, revise one or the other.

---

### 2.2 State of the Art

Survey the existing research relevant to your topic. This is not a book-by-book or article-by-article summary — it is a **thematic synthesis**. Group studies by what they find, debate, or assume, and evaluate them critically.

- Organise **thematically**, not chronologically (unless the history of the field is your argument)
- Evaluate quality: note sample sizes, methodological limitations, context-specificity
- Show you understand what is **contested**, not just what is established
- Cite primarily peer-reviewed work; where grey literature is appropriate (policy docs, clinical guidelines), justify its inclusion
- Use databases suited to your field: PsycINFO, PubMed, CINAHL, Social Care Online, JSTOR

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** The "annotated bibliography" trap — listing what each author said without synthesising across sources. Your voice should organise the literature, not the other way around.

---

### 2.3 Gap Statement

The most critical paragraph of the literature review. It is the logical culmination of everything you have mapped above. Based on what the field has and has not done, you now establish the specific, justified gap your thesis addresses.

- The gap should **follow directly** from your critical evaluation — not appear as a surprise
- Be precise: a gap is not *"no one has studied this"* — it is *"this mechanism has not been studied in this population / using this method / within this theoretical frame"*
- The gap statement leads directly into — and should mirror — your research question

> 💡 **Key question:** Does your gap statement make the reader think *"of course — this is exactly what needs investigating next"*? That is the standard you are aiming for.

## 3. Methodology

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count
> *(MSc: ~2,500–4,000 words · PhD: ~8,000–15,000 words)*

The methodology chapter is your **scholarly transparency**. It tells the reader exactly how you investigated your question, and — crucially — *why* those choices were the right ones. An examiner should be able to replicate your study from this chapter alone.

---

### 3.1 Design

State your overall research design: quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods. Identify the specific design type within that category.

| Type | Examples |
|------|---------|
| **Qualitative** | Thematic analysis, grounded theory, phenomenology, discourse analysis, case study, ethnography |
| **Quantitative** | RCT, survey, longitudinal cohort, cross-sectional, quasi-experimental |
| **Mixed** | Specify the integration strategy: concurrent, sequential, or embedded |

Also describe your **epistemological position** (interpretivist, positivist, constructivist, critical realist) — this connects directly back to your theoretical framework.

> 📝 **Tip:** In Disability Studies and Counselling Psychology, the design and epistemological position are often the heart of the scholarly contribution. Do not treat them as administrative boxes to tick.

---

### 3.2 Method

The step-by-step account of what you actually did. Be precise — this section should have enough detail that another researcher could replicate your study.

- **Participants / sample:** who, how many, how recruited, inclusion and exclusion criteria
- **Data collection:** instruments used (interview schedule, survey, observation protocol), how administered, where, when
- **Ethical considerations:** ethics approval, consent process, anonymisation, right to withdraw
- **Data analysis:** the specific procedure used to move from raw data to findings (e.g. Braun & Clarke's six-phase thematic analysis; SPSS procedure; NVivo coding protocol)

---

### 3.3 Justification

Defend every significant methodological choice. Do not just describe what you did — explain why it was the best available option for your question.

- Why this design rather than an alternative?
- Why this sample size / sampling strategy?
- Why this specific analytic approach?
- Cite **methodological literature** — not just substantive literature — to support your choices

> 💡 **Key question:** For every method choice, ask: *"What is the alternative, and why is my choice better suited to my question?"* That reasoning belongs here.

---

### 3.4 Limitations

Acknowledge the known constraints of your approach honestly and analytically. This is not weakness — it is scholarly rigour.

- **Scope limitations:** what your study cannot generalise to
- **Method limitations:** what your chosen design cannot capture
- **Practical limitations:** sample access, time, resources
- For each limitation, briefly note how you mitigated it or why it does not invalidate your findings

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Do not over-apologise for limitations, and do not hide them. Frame them as the honest boundary conditions of your contribution — this is what experienced researchers do.

---

## 4. Results

> **Typical length:** 15–25% of total word count
> *Length varies greatly by method and data volume.*

Results is the most **rule-governed** section of your thesis. Its single job is to present what you found, organised in a way that directly maps to your research question and your methods. **Interpretation belongs in Discussion — not here.**

---

### 4.1 Presenting Your Data

The organisation of your results should mirror the logic of your research question and the structure of your analysis.

| Method | How to present |
|--------|---------------|
| **Qualitative** | Present themes or categories with illustrative quotes. Each theme needs: a label, a description, and evidence from your data |
| **Quantitative** | Present statistics in logical order (descriptive first, then inferential). Use tables and figures, but narrate what they show — do not simply dump numbers |
| **Mixed methods** | Report qualitative and quantitative results separately before integrating |

**The cardinal rule:** Every result you report must connect to your research question. If a finding does not relate to your question, it does not belong in Results.

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** *"Participants reported high levels of anxiety"* is a result. *"This suggests the intervention was insufficient"* is an interpretation — it belongs in Discussion.

> 📝 **Tip:** Structure your Results section using your themes or sub-questions as subheadings. This creates a clear audit trail from question → findings → discussion.

---

## 5. Discussion

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count
> *Often the most intellectually demanding section to write.*

Discussion is where your **scholarly voice is loudest**. This is where you interpret what your results mean, connect them back to the literature you reviewed, explore what was surprising, and articulate what your findings actually contribute to the field.

---

### 5.1 Interpretation, Literature Links, and Implications

Work through your results systematically. For each major finding:

1. **Interpret** — what does this finding mean within your theoretical framework?
2. **Link** — how does it confirm, challenge, extend, or contradict the existing literature?
3. **Imply** — what are the broader implications: theoretical, clinical, policy, social?

Also use this section to:
- Address **unexpected or negative findings** — these are often your most interesting contributions
- Revisit your theoretical framework and reflect on whether findings support, complicate, or require revision of it
- Note the boundaries of your interpretation — what you *cannot* claim from your data

> 💡 **Key question:** For every result, ask: *"Why does this matter beyond my own study?"* That answer is your contribution to the field.

> 📝 **Tip:** In Disability Studies and Counselling Psych especially, the Discussion is the place for **reflexivity** — examining how your positionality shaped your interpretations. This is not optional in these fields; it is a mark of sophistication.

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Do not simply repeat your results in the Discussion. Each paragraph should advance the interpretive argument, not recap the data.

## 6. Conclusion

> **Typical length:** 3–7% of total word count
> *(MSc: ~600–1,000 words · PhD: ~2,000–4,000 words)*

The conclusion closes the contract you opened in the Introduction. It directly answers your research question, states your original contribution to knowledge, and opens the horizon for future research. It should feel like an **arrival** — not a summary.

---

### 6.1 Answer, Contributions, and Future Directions

Structure your conclusion around three moves:

1. **Answer the research question** — State directly and explicitly what your thesis found. Do not hedge excessively — you have earned the right to make a claim.

2. **State your contributions** — What does this thesis add that was not there before? This might be:
   - *Empirical* — new data
   - *Theoretical* — a new framework or modification
   - *Methodological* — a new approach
   - *Practical* — evidence for a new intervention or policy

3. **Future directions** — What questions does your work open up? What should researchers investigate next? What would you do differently or extend if you continued?

> 💡 **Key question:** Can you finish this sentence clearly and confidently: *"This thesis demonstrates that…"*? That sentence is your conclusion's core.

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Do not introduce new evidence or new arguments in the Conclusion. If something belongs here, it should have appeared in Results or Discussion first.

> 📝 **Tip:** The Introduction and Conclusion should read as a matched pair. A reader who reads only those two sections should understand exactly what you studied, why, and what you found.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Recommended Writing Order

Counterintuitively, the order you *write* a thesis is not the order it is *read*. Most experienced academic writers use this sequence:

| Order | Section | Why |
|-------|---------|-----|
| 1 | **Research Question** | Everything else is built on this. Refine it first. |
| 2 | **Methodology** | Write while doing the research — capture decisions in real time. |
| 3 | **Results** | Systematic and descriptive — write while the data is freshest. |
| 4 | **Literature Review** | You now know what literature is actually relevant to your findings. |
| 5 | **Discussion** | Requires Results and Literature Review to be substantially complete. |
| 6 | **Introduction** | Write last — you now know exactly what your thesis is about. |
| 7 | **Conclusion** | Write after Introduction — mirror and close the contract. |
| 8 | **Abstract** | Write very last — it summarises a complete work. |

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

Use before submitting any chapter draft, and again for the full thesis.

- [ ] Research question is stated clearly in the Introduction and answered directly in the Conclusion
- [ ] Every chapter connects logically to the one before and after it
- [ ] Literature Review synthesises thematically — not source by source
- [ ] Gap statement follows from the critical evaluation of literature
- [ ] Methodology justifies every major design choice with methodological literature
- [ ] Results section contains no interpretation (interpretation is in Discussion only)
- [ ] Every result links back to the research question
- [ ] Discussion connects each finding to the existing literature
- [ ] Limitations are acknowledged with mitigation noted
- [ ] Conclusion directly states the answer to the research question
- [ ] Original contributions are explicitly named in the Conclusion
- [ ] Introduction and Conclusion read as a coherent matched pair
- [ ] Abstract is written last and covers: aim, methods, findings, conclusions
- [ ] References are complete, consistently formatted, and include all cited works
- [ ] Word count is within the range specified by your institution

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
Emphasis on mastery of existing knowledge and rigorous application of a method. Original empirical contribution is valued but less expected than at PhD level. Theoretical sophistication and critical engagement with literature are central markers of a strong thesis.

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
The Discussion section typically carries extra weight — clinical reflection, ethical reasoning, and reflexivity about your own positionality are frequently assessed. Your theoretical framework is often deeply personalised and must be clearly justified.

#### Diploma (e.g. Art Therapy)
Practitioner-research integration is key. How does your empirical work relate to practice? Your Limitations section often includes explicit reflexive discussion of your dual role as practitioner and researcher.

#### PhD (e.g. Clinical Psych / Public Health / Social Science)
Original contribution to knowledge is **mandatory** — not aspirational. Your gap statement, theoretical framework, and Discussion must clearly articulate what the field now knows that it did not before your thesis. Examiners will ask directly: *"What is new here?"*
