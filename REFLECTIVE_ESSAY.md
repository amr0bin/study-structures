# 🪞 Reflective Essay

> **A complete reference for MA, Diploma, and PhD reflective writing**
> A reflective essay at postgraduate level is not journalling. It has a formal structure, is assessed on the quality of critical analysis, and requires you to move between personal experience and theoretical engagement throughout. The personal is the starting point — not the destination.

## 1. Choosing a Reflective Framework

> **Most programmes specify a framework — use the one required**

Reflective frameworks are scaffolds, not straitjackets. They provide a structure that moves you from description through to analytical conclusion and action. The three most common in health, social science, and counselling programmes are:

| Framework | Stages | Best for |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Gibbs (1988)** | Description → Feelings → Evaluation → Analysis → Conclusion → Action Plan | General professional practice; placement reflection |
| **Kolb (1984)** | Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualisation → Active Experimentation | Learning from experience in any context |
| **Johns (2000)** | Description → Reflection → Influencing factors → Could I have dealt better → Learning | Clinical and nursing contexts; structured self-questioning |

This guide follows the **Gibbs cycle** — the most commonly required in your pathway. The principles apply across frameworks.

> 📝 **Tip:** The framework is a process, not a template. Your reflection should feel like a continuous essay, not six labelled sections. The stages give you a sequence to move through — not a form to fill in.

## 2. Description

> **Typical length:** 10–15% of total word count
> *Keep this brief — the marks are not here*

Describe what happened. This section is factual and concrete — no interpretation, no feelings, no analysis yet. The purpose is to establish the shared reference point from which the reflection will move.

---

### 2.1 What to Include

- What was the situation? Where, when, who was involved?
- What did you do? What did others do?
- What was the outcome?

### 2.2 What to Exclude

- Your feelings about it (that's the next section)
- Your interpretation of why it happened (that's analysis)
- What you think you should have done (that's conclusion and action plan)

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Long descriptions that consume most of your word count. A description that runs to 600 words in a 2,000-word essay has left almost no room for analysis — which is where the marks are. Two to three paragraphs is usually sufficient.

---

## 3. Feelings

> **Typical length:** 8–12% of total word count

This section asks for your emotional and somatic response to the experience. At postgraduate level in counselling and therapeutic fields, emotional literacy is itself a professional competency — the quality of your feelings section reflects your capacity for self-awareness.

---

### 3.1 Naming Feelings Precisely

There is a significant difference between "I felt uncomfortable" and "I noticed a tightening in my chest that I later recognised as anxiety about appearing incompetent in front of my supervisor." Precision matters.

- Use specific emotional vocabulary — not just "good," "bad," "uncomfortable," "fine"
- Name the feelings as they occurred in sequence, if they shifted
- Include somatic experience where relevant — what did you notice in your body?
- Note feelings that surprised you or that you were reluctant to acknowledge

> 💡 **Key question:** Are you naming the feeling you actually had, or the feeling it is more comfortable to report? The most important feelings in a placement or practice context are often the ones that are hardest to name — ambivalence, irritation, fear, helplessness. These are not failures; they are data.

---

## 4. Evaluation

> **Typical length:** 8–10% of total word count

Evaluate the experience — what went well, and what was difficult or did not go as you would have wished. This is still relatively close to the experience, but begins to introduce some assessment.

---

### 4.1 What Worked

- What aspects of your practice, response, or decision-making were effective?
- What went better than you expected?
- What in the situation was handled well — by you or others?

---

### 4.2 What Was Difficult

- What did not go as intended?
- Where did you feel uncertain, stuck, or reactive?
- What was the impact of what went less well — on you, on others, on the situation?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Evaluations that only describe difficulty without any acknowledgement of what worked, or conversely, that are so positive they lack critical honesty. Balanced evaluation is a mark of professional maturity.

---

## 5. Analysis

> **Typical length:** 40–50% of total word count
> *This is where the essay is won or lost*

Analysis is the section where theory meets experience. It moves from "what happened and how I felt about it" to "what this experience reveals about professional practice, the field, myself as a developing practitioner, and the theoretical frameworks I am working within."

---

### 5.1 Connecting to Theory

- Which theoretical frameworks, models, or concepts illuminate this experience?
- What does the theory predict would happen in a situation like this — and did it?
- Where does the theory fall short of accounting for what actually occurred?
- Cite the literature — a reflective essay is still an academic essay

> 📝 **Tip:** The strongest analytical move is to use theory to explain *why* the experience unfolded as it did — not just to label it. "What I experienced could be understood as countertransference (Freud, 1910; Heimann, 1950) — specifically, my difficulty tolerating the client's hopelessness may have activated my own" is analysis. "I experienced countertransference" is a label.

---

### 5.2 Examining Assumptions

Analysis at postgraduate level means examining the assumptions that shaped your response:

- What did you assume going into the situation — about the client, the context, your role?
- Which assumptions were confirmed, and which were disrupted?
- What do these assumptions reveal about your theoretical orientation, cultural position, or professional formation?

---

### 5.3 Reflexivity — Your Positionality

In counselling, disability studies, and art therapy contexts, reflexivity is integral to the analysis.

- How did your own history, identity, or background shape how you responded?
- What in you was activated by this particular situation?
- What might a client, colleague, or supervisor see in your response that you cannot easily see yourself?

> 💡 **Key question:** What does this experience reveal about you as a developing practitioner — not just about the situation? Analysis that stays entirely outside the self has not met the standard of reflexive practice.

---

### 5.4 Multiple Perspectives

Strong analysis considers the experience from more than one perspective:

- How might the client (or service user, student, colleague) have experienced the same situation?
- How might a more experienced practitioner have responded, and why?
- What institutional or systemic factors shaped what was possible in this situation?

---

## 6. Conclusion

> **Typical length:** 8–10% of total word count

The conclusion answers: what have you learned? Not just "I learned X" but what this experience has revealed about yourself, your practice, and the field you are entering.

---

### 6.1 What the Conclusion Must Include

1. **A direct statement of learning** — what you understand now that you did not before
2. **Theoretical integration** — how the experience has deepened, complicated, or revised your understanding of the frameworks you are working within
3. **Professional development** — what this means for you as a developing practitioner

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Conclusions that are vague ("This experience taught me a lot about myself") or circular ("I learned that reflection is important"). Be specific about what, precisely, you learned — and what evidence from the experience supports that learning.

---

## 7. Action Plan

> **Typical length:** 8–10% of total word count**

The action plan answers: what will you do differently, or do more of, as a result of this reflection? It should be concrete, realistic, and connected to the analysis.

---

### 7.1 What an Action Plan Is

- Specific changes to practice, approach, or self-monitoring
- Identified areas for further learning, reading, or supervision attention
- Realistic commitments: not "I will always be more empathic" but "I will bring this dynamic to supervision and explore what was activated in me"

> 📝 **Tip:** The action plan is where reflexive practice becomes professional development. The quality of an action plan reveals whether the reflection was genuine or performative. Vague intentions indicate that the analysis has not led anywhere.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ The Posture of Reflection

Postgraduate reflective writing requires what the philosopher Hans-Georg Gadamer called a "readiness to be disturbed" — a willingness to have your assumptions and responses examined, rather than simply reported. This is harder than it sounds in an assessed piece.

Write the first draft freely and honestly. Then revise for academic register — adding citations, sharpening analysis, ensuring the structure holds — without losing the honesty of the first draft.

### ✍️ Recommended Writing Order

| Order | Step | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | **Write freely** | Get the experience and your response on the page without worrying about structure |
| 2 | **Identify the most significant moment** | What is the reflection really about? |
| 3 | **Find the relevant literature** | What theory illuminates this? |
| 4 | **Draft the analysis** | The heart of the essay |
| 5 | **Draft description, feelings, evaluation** | Now you know what they are building toward |
| 6 | **Draft conclusion and action plan** | What the analysis leads to |
| 7 | **Revise the whole** | Does it read as a coherent essay, not six separate sections? |

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Reflective framework is identified (Gibbs, Kolb, Johns, or programme-specified)
- [ ] Description is factual and brief — no interpretation in this section
- [ ] Feelings are named specifically — not just "uncomfortable" or "good"
- [ ] Evaluation addresses both what worked and what was difficult
- [ ] Analysis connects to relevant theoretical literature with citations
- [ ] Analysis examines assumptions, not just events
- [ ] Reflexivity is present — your positionality is examined, not just mentioned
- [ ] Multiple perspectives are considered in the analysis
- [ ] Conclusion states specific learning — not vague "I learned a lot"
- [ ] Action plan is concrete and realistic
- [ ] Essay reads as a continuous piece — not six labelled boxes
- [ ] All citations in APA 7th
- [ ] Confidentiality maintained — any real people or situations appropriately anonymised

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
Reflective essays in counselling training carry clinical weight — the quality of your self-examination is a direct indicator of your capacity for therapeutic practice. Supervisors often read these essays alongside placement reports. The reflexivity section is particularly important.

#### Diploma (e.g. Art Therapy)
In art therapy training, the reflective essay often includes reflection on your own art-making process and its relationship to your developing therapeutic identity. Integrating the creative and the analytical is itself the task.

#### PhD
At PhD level, reflective writing appears in methodology chapters (reflexivity about your positionality as a researcher) and in viva preparation. The skills developed in earlier reflective assignments directly inform your capacity to account for your own role in the research.
