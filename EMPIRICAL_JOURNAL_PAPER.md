# 📄 Empirical Journal-Format Paper

> **A complete reference for MSc and PhD journal-format assignments**
> Writing in the format of a peer-reviewed journal article is a distinct skill from thesis writing. It is tightly wordcounted, structured for a specialist audience, and written as a contribution to a field — not as an explanation for an examiner. The goal is a paper that could, in principle, be submitted for publication.

## The Journal Paper vs the Thesis Chapter

Before writing, understand how these differ:

| Feature | Journal paper | Thesis chapter |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| Audience | Specialist peers in the field | An examiner assessing your development |
| Word count | Strict (usually 5,000–8,000) | More flexible |
| Tone | Authoritative, concise | Can be more expansive and discursive |
| Background | Assumes significant field knowledge | Explains more context |
| Contribution | Must state a clear original contribution | Can be part of a larger whole |
| Limitations | Stated briefly and precisely | Can be more detailed |

> 📝 **Tip:** Before writing, identify the journal you are targeting (or the journal your assignment is modelled on) and read its author guidelines. Word limits, abstract structure, and reference style vary by journal. If the assignment does not specify a target journal, use APA 7th and standard IMRD structure.

## 1. Abstract

> **Typical length:** 150–250 words · written last

The abstract is the most-read section of any paper — and the section that determines whether anyone reads the rest. It must be self-contained: a reader should understand your study, findings, and conclusions from the abstract alone.

---

### 1.1 Standard Abstract Structure (APA format)

| Element | What to include | Approximate length |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Background / Objective** | Why this study was conducted; the research question or hypothesis | 2–3 sentences |
| **Method** | Design, participants, procedure, measures | 2–3 sentences |
| **Results** | Key findings, with statistical or thematic detail | 2–3 sentences |
| **Conclusions** | What the findings mean; implications | 1–2 sentences |

Some journals use structured abstracts with explicit headings; others use unstructured paragraphs. Follow the target journal's convention.

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Abstracts that describe what the paper *does* ("This paper examines...") rather than what it *found* ("Findings indicated that..."). An abstract is a report of results, not a table of contents.

---

## 2. Introduction

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count
> *Funnel structure: broad → specific → your study*

The introduction moves from the broader field to the specific gap, arriving at your aims and hypotheses or research questions. It is tighter than a thesis literature review — you are not mapping the whole field, you are justifying this study.

---

### 2.1 Structure of the Introduction

1. **Open with the broader problem or phenomenon** — establish why this area matters (2–3 paragraphs)
2. **Survey the relevant literature** — what is known, and from what kinds of evidence? Focus on what is directly relevant to your study, not everything in the field
3. **Identify the specific gap** — what has not been done, and why does it matter?
4. **State your aims** — and, if applicable, hypotheses or research questions

> 💡 **Key question:** Could a specialist reader predict your method from your introduction? If the gap and aims clearly point toward a specific design, you have written a tight introduction. If the connection between the gap and your method is unclear, revise.

---

### 2.2 Citing the Literature

In a journal paper, every empirical claim needs a citation. Broad, uncited claims ("mental health is increasingly important") are not acceptable.

- Cite primary sources — original studies, not review papers citing them
- Use recent citations unless a foundational older work is essential
- Avoid over-citation of consensus findings; cite selectively and purposefully

## 3. Method

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count
> *Detailed enough for another researcher to replicate your study*

The method section is a transparent account of exactly what you did. Readers should be able to assess the rigour of your study and, in principle, replicate it.

---

### 3.1 Standard Subsections

**Participants**
- How many participants? (Report both target and achieved sample)
- Recruitment method and setting
- Inclusion and exclusion criteria
- Relevant demographic characteristics (mean age, gender, clinical characteristics)
- Ethical approval: institution name, approval number, and consent procedure

**Materials / Measures**
- All instruments, questionnaires, interview schedules, or stimuli used
- For validated measures: name, reference, what it measures, reliability data (Cronbach's α or equivalent)
- For bespoke materials: brief description; include as appendix if required

**Procedure**
- Exactly what happened, in sequence: recruitment → consent → data collection → debrief
- Any randomisation, blinding, or counterbalancing
- For qualitative studies: the interview or data collection procedure; how data were recorded

**Data Analysis**
- For quantitative: statistical tests used, software, significance threshold (usually *p* < .05), approach to missing data
- For qualitative: the specific analytic approach (e.g. "Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-phase reflexive thematic analysis"), software if used, how rigour was ensured

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Method sections that describe what you *planned* rather than what you *did*, or that omit information necessary for replication. The method is a transparent record, not a proposal.

## 4. Results

> **Typical length:** 20–25% of total word count
> *Findings only — interpretation belongs in the Discussion*

The results section presents your findings clearly and completely, without interpretation. In a journal paper, results are often densely presented — the text narrates the findings, tables and figures carry the detail.

---

### 4.1 For Quantitative Results

- Begin with descriptive statistics before inferential
- Report test statistics, degrees of freedom, *p* values, and effect sizes: *t*(58) = 2.34, *p* = .022, *d* = 0.61
- Use tables for complex data; use figures for trends or comparisons
- Report all pre-specified outcomes — do not selectively report only significant results

**APA statistical reporting format:**
- Mean and SD: *M* = 4.32, *SD* = 0.87
- Correlation: *r*(48) = .42, *p* = .003
- *t*-test: *t*(62) = 3.14, *p* = .002, *d* = 0.79
- ANOVA: *F*(2, 87) = 5.43, *p* = .006, η² = .11

---

### 4.2 For Qualitative Results

- Present themes with: a theme name, a descriptive account of what the theme captures, and illustrative quotations
- Quotes should be verbatim, indented if over 40 words, with a participant identifier
- Analytic commentary after each quote explains *what* the quote demonstrates — do not let quotes speak for themselves

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Results sections that interpret findings. "Participants reported high levels of anxiety, *which suggests that the intervention was insufficient*" — the italicised portion is interpretation and belongs in Discussion.

---

## 5. Discussion

> **Typical length:** 25–30% of total word count**

The discussion interprets your results, connects them to the existing literature, considers implications, acknowledges limitations, and concludes.

---

### 5.1 Structure of the Discussion

1. **Restate your main finding** — in one or two sentences, without statistics; a plain-language summary of what you found
2. **Interpret each finding** — what do they mean? Refer back to the literature introduced in the introduction
3. **Unexpected or null findings** — if something did not come out as hypothesised, analyse why
4. **Theoretical implications** — what do your findings contribute to theory or understanding?
5. **Practical implications** — for practice, policy, or intervention
6. **Limitations** — be specific: sample characteristics, design constraints, measurement limitations
7. **Future research** — what should be done next, and why?
8. **Conclusion** — a final 1–2 sentence statement of the study's contribution

> 📝 **Tip:** The discussion should move through findings, literature, and implications smoothly — not as labelled subsections (unless the journal requires them). Avoid introducing new evidence in the discussion.

---

## 6. References

All references in APA 7th format. In a journal paper, references should be:

- Complete and accurate — every in-text citation has a reference; every reference has an in-text citation
- Primary sources wherever possible
- Recent (within the last 10 years) except for foundational or classic works
- Formatted consistently — use Zotero to manage this

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Targeting a Journal

If your assignment invites you to target a specific journal:

1. Identify 2–3 journals that publish work in your topic area and methodological approach
2. Read the author guidelines for word limits, abstract format, reference style
3. Read recent issues — what does a typical paper in this journal look like?
4. Write in a register and level of detail consistent with published papers in the journal

### ✍️ Recommended Writing Order

| Order | Step | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | **Method** | You know exactly what you did |
| 2 | **Results** | Report what you found |
| 3 | **Introduction** | Now you know what your study justifies |
| 4 | **Discussion** | Interpret what you found |
| 5 | **Abstract** | Summarise the complete paper |
| 6 | **Title** | Precise, informative, under 12 words |

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Abstract is self-contained: background, method, results, conclusions
- [ ] Introduction moves from broad to specific to your study
- [ ] Gap is clearly identified and your study directly addresses it
- [ ] Method is detailed enough for replication
- [ ] Ethical approval and consent procedure are stated
- [ ] Measures are described with reliability data
- [ ] Results report descriptive then inferential statistics (quantitative) or themes with quotes (qualitative)
- [ ] Results contain no interpretation
- [ ] Statistical reporting follows APA format (for quantitative)
- [ ] Discussion begins with a plain-language summary of findings
- [ ] Each finding is connected to the existing literature
- [ ] Limitations are specific and honest
- [ ] Future research directions are stated
- [ ] All citations in APA 7th, complete and consistent
- [ ] Word count is within the target journal or assignment limit

---

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc
Journal-format assignments at MSc level often use a real dataset from a lab or programme. Focus on accurate reporting and clear interpretation — the contribution may be modest and that is appropriate.

#### PhD
At PhD level, the journal paper is often a publishable piece — or part of a "papers model" thesis where two to three published or publishable papers constitute the thesis body. At this level, the contribution claim in your introduction and discussion must be genuinely original.
