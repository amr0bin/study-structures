# 🔍 Case Study — Social Science & Policy

> **A complete reference for MSc and PhD case study assignments**
> A case study is an in-depth analysis of a bounded instance — a person, organisation, community, policy, or event — examined through a theoretical lens. The goal is always to generate insight that extends beyond the single case: the case is a window into something larger.

## 1. Introduction

> **Typical length:** 10–15% of total word count
> *(2,000-word case study: ~250 words · 5,000-word: ~600 words)*

The introduction does three things: it presents the case, it states your analytical question, and it names the theoretical framework through which you will examine it. The reader should know all three before the first section begins.

---

### 1.1 Presenting the Case

Introduce the case briefly — enough to orient the reader, but not so much that you pre-empt the context section.

- Name the case: what is it, who is involved, what is its significance?
- Establish why this case is worth examining — what makes it analytically interesting or representative?
- A strong case study is chosen because it illuminates something beyond itself: a typical case (representative of a broader pattern), a deviant case (an exception that reveals the limits of a general rule), or a critical case (where the outcome is particularly telling)

> 💡 **Key question:** Why *this* case? A case study that could have been any case — where the specific choice is arbitrary — has not been well designed. The case selection should be theoretically or empirically motivated.

---

### 1.2 The Analytical Question

Your case study investigates a specific question — not just "what happened" but "what does what happened reveal about X?"

- State the question explicitly
- The question should be answerable through examination of the case
- It should point toward the theoretical framework: "How does the social model of disability account for..." or "What does this policy reveal about the assumptions embedded in..."

---

### 1.3 The Theoretical Lens

Name the framework through which you will examine the case. This is not decoration — it determines what you notice, what counts as significant, and how you interpret what you find.

- Name the framework and its key claims (briefly — a sentence or two)
- Justify why it is appropriate for this case and this question
- Note if there is a tension between alternative frameworks — this can itself become part of your analysis

---

## 2. Context

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count

Context is the background a reader needs to make sense of your analysis. It is not the analysis itself. Be selective — include what is necessary, not everything that is available.

---

### 2.1 What Context to Include

| Type of context | What it covers |
|----------------|---------------|
| **Historical** | How the situation came to be; the sequence of events leading to the case |
| **Social / structural** | The social conditions, power relations, or systemic factors that shape the case |
| **Policy / legal** | Relevant legislation, policy frameworks, or institutional rules |
| **Theoretical** | How the framework has been applied to similar cases — what it would predict |

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Context sections that become mini-history essays. Every sentence of context should be necessary for the reader to follow the analysis that follows. If removing a paragraph doesn't affect the analysis, the paragraph doesn't belong.

---

## 3. Analysis

> **Typical length:** 40–45% of total word count
> *The intellectual heart of the case study*

This is where the theoretical framework meets the case data. Apply your lens systematically — showing what the framework reveals about the case, where the case confirms or complicates the framework, and where tensions or surprises emerge.

---

### 3.1 Structuring the Analysis

Organise by themes or analytical questions, not by chronology or by the events of the case.

- Each analytical section should make a claim — not just describe what happened, but argue what it means
- Use the theoretical framework actively: "From a critical disability studies perspective, this policy positions disabled people as recipients rather than agents of change..."
- Bring in evidence from the case (documents, statistics, accounts, reports) and interpret it through the lens
- Note where the case data challenges or extends the theoretical framework — this is often where the most interesting analysis lives

> 📝 **Tip:** A useful test: if you removed all theoretical language from your analysis, would it still be analysis — or would it become description? The theory is not a label applied to facts; it is a lens that reveals what the facts mean.

---

### 3.2 Handling Complexity and Contradiction

Real cases are rarely neat. Evidence points in different directions; actors have conflicting interests; the framework does not explain everything.

- Do not smooth over complexity in the service of a clean argument
- Acknowledge where the evidence is ambiguous or contradictory — and analyse *why* it is
- Note where the theoretical framework has explanatory limits for this particular case

> 💡 **Key question:** What does this case show that your framework *cannot* fully explain? That gap is often your most interesting finding.

---

## 4. Discussion

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count

The discussion lifts the analysis from the specific case to the broader implications. What does this case reveal about the phenomenon you are studying?

---

### 4.1 From Case to Claim

A case study is not an end in itself — it is a means of generating insight about something beyond the case. The discussion makes this move explicit.

- What does the case reveal about the broader social, policy, or theoretical landscape?
- How does it confirm, challenge, extend, or complicate existing literature on this phenomenon?
- What are the implications — for policy, for practice, for theory, for the people affected?

---

### 4.2 Generalisation from a Single Case

A single case study cannot produce statistical generalisation. But it can produce:

- **Analytical generalisation:** using the case to develop, test, or refine theoretical propositions
- **Naturalistic generalisation:** enabling readers who recognise the case to apply insights to their own contexts
- **Conceptual contribution:** showing that a concept or framework does or does not operate as predicted

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Claims that overreach the evidence. "This case demonstrates that all disability policy in Canada is deficit-based" is not warranted by a single case. "This case illustrates how deficit-based assumptions can be embedded in apparently progressive policy frameworks, which has implications for..." is appropriately scoped.

---

## 5. Conclusion

> **Typical length:** 8–10% of total word count

---

### 5.1 What the Conclusion Must Do

1. **Answer your analytical question** — directly, in light of the analysis
2. **State the implications** — for theory, policy, or practice
3. **Acknowledge the limits of single-case analysis** — what this case cannot tell us, and what further research would be needed

> 📝 **Tip:** The conclusion of a case study is often where its contribution becomes clearest. Be confident in stating what you have found — and honest about the boundaries of that finding.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Selecting a Case

The case selection is a methodological decision. Before writing, be able to answer:

- Is this a typical, deviant, critical, or exploratory case — and why does that matter for your argument?
- Is the case bounded — does it have clear edges in time, place, or membership?
- Do you have access to sufficient evidence to examine it rigorously?
- Will the case generate insight beyond itself?

### ✍️ Recommended Writing Order

| Order | Step | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | **Define the analytical question** | Everything else follows from this |
| 2 | **Select and justify the theoretical framework** | Determines what evidence is relevant |
| 3 | **Gather case evidence** | Documents, data, accounts, reports |
| 4 | **Draft the analysis** | The framework meeting the evidence |
| 5 | **Draft the discussion** | From case to broader claim |
| 6 | **Draft introduction and context** | Written last so they introduce what you actually wrote |

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Case is introduced with clear rationale for its selection
- [ ] Analytical question is stated explicitly in the introduction
- [ ] Theoretical framework is named, explained, and justified
- [ ] Context is selective and necessary — not exhaustive background
- [ ] Analysis applies the framework actively to the case data
- [ ] Analysis makes claims, not just descriptions
- [ ] Complexity and contradiction in the case are acknowledged
- [ ] Discussion moves from the specific case to broader implications
- [ ] Claims are appropriately scoped — not overgeneralised from a single case
- [ ] Conclusion answers the analytical question directly
- [ ] Limitations of single-case analysis are acknowledged
- [ ] All sources cited in APA 7th

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
Case studies in disability studies often examine a policy, an institutional practice, or a community — examined through frameworks like the social model, critical disability theory, or intersectionality. The analytical question is typically about how structural factors shape the experiences or outcomes of disabled people.

#### PhD
At PhD level, case study may be your primary research methodology. In that context, refer to Yin's *Case Study Research* (2018) or Stake's *The Art of Case Study Research* (1995) for the full methodological framework — this guide covers the assignment format, not the methodology at full research depth.
