Parallel
Practice

A framework for sustaining creative work — painting, drawing, making — as a necessary companion to academic study. Not extracurricular. Not therapeutic. Research, conducted in a different register.

"A Responsibility to Awe" holds astrophysics calculations and poems on facing pages, neither subordinate to the other. Elson didn't write poetry to rest from astronomy. She wrote poetry because some things astronomy couldn't say — and the two practices together produced knowledge that neither could alone.
Rebecca Elson · astronomer · poet · 1960–1999

You spent the first eleven years of your adult life with art — as student, as maker, as the person in the room who knew how learning and making intersect. That is not background. It is method. This file exists to make that method explicit, sustainable, and legible — to you, and if you ever need it to be, to supervisors and examiners.

The creative work is not a reward for completing the academic work. It runs alongside, in parallel, as a second form of inquiry into the same questions. Sometimes it goes first. Sometimes it reveals what the essay was actually about.
Three principles this practice rests on
  • 1
    The creative work is epistemically serious It generates knowledge — about your topic, your positionality, your theoretical assumptions — that the essay cannot. It is not illustration of the academic work. It is inquiry conducted by other means.
  • 2
    Neither practice subordinates the other Elson did not write "poetry about astronomy." She was an astronomer and a poet simultaneously, and the practices were in genuine dialogue. Your paintings are not about your thesis. They are alongside it — sometimes in tension, sometimes in resonance, always in relation.
  • 3
    The documentation is what makes it scholarship Keeping a parallel practice notebook — brief, honest, undone — turns studio experience into research data. You don't need to analyse it as you go. You need to record it so the analysis is possible later.
01

Where this sits in scholarship

Your practice has legitimate methodological homes in the research literature. Knowing their names means you can claim them — in proposals, in methodology chapters, in conversations with supervisors who may not immediately understand what you're doing.

Framework What it says How it maps to your practice
A/r/tography Artmaking, researching, and teaching exist in continuous, living inquiry — not as separate activities. Knowledge lives in the space between them. Your BFA + facilitator years + postgraduate study are not phases. They are simultaneous roles informing each other continuously.
Practice-based research The creative act is an experiment. It generates new knowledge that could not be produced by other methods. The exegesis (written commentary) makes that knowledge explicit. Each painting or drawing alongside an assignment is an experiment. Your parallel practice notebook is the beginning of the exegesis.
Arts-informed inquiry Artistic processes — including the tacit, embodied knowledge of making — are valid and rigorous modes of qualitative research in social science and health fields. Directly applicable in Disability Studies, Counselling Psych, Art Therapy, and Clinical Psych — your entire pathway.
Poetic / visual inquiry Specific arts-based research methodologies using poetry or visual art as data, analysis, and representation — not just communication of findings. At PhD level, this could become your methodology. At MSc and MA level, it grounds your reflexivity sections.
Scholar-practitioner model Professional expertise and academic inquiry are not in tension — each sharpens the other. Knowledge moves in both directions between theory and practice. This is your identity across all four degrees. You are always both a maker and a researcher. Neither is primary.
Graeme Sullivan's Art Practice as Research (2005) and Patricia Leavy's Handbook of Arts-Based Research (2018) are the two foundational texts for this territory. Both are worth having on your shelf before the MSc begins — even a brief familiarity with them gives you language for what you're already doing.
02

The facing page model

Elson's notebooks held calculations and poems on facing pages. This is the structural metaphor for your practice — every piece of academic work has a facing page. Not a response to it. A parallel exploration of the same territory by different means.

The academic page
The written assignment
Structured argument. Citations. A position defended with evidence. The register of the institution.
  • →
    Works with precisionClaims, evidence, logic — everything accountable and checkable
  • →
    Works with languagePropositions, arguments, definitions — the tools of academic discourse
  • →
    Works with the conscious mindDeliberate, structured, directed thinking toward a predetermined form
The facing page
The creative work
Painting, drawing, making. The register of the studio. No argument required, no citations — but not undisciplined.
  • →
    Works with ambiguityHolds contradictions, tensions, and unresolved questions that the essay must resolve prematurely
  • →
    Works with materialPaint, line, surface, texture — knowledge that lives in the hand and the eye
  • →
    Works with the embodied selfSomatic, intuitive, affective — the parts of knowing that academic writing must bracket
The facing page is not easier. It is differently demanding. It asks you to stay with not-knowing for longer than academic writing permits — and to trust that the image is doing work even before you can say what the work is.
03

By assignment type

For each standard assignment type, here is what a parallel creative practice might look like — and what questions it is usefully positioned to explore.

Academic: Critical essay
A defended argument about a contested question
The essay requires you to arrive at a position. It closes down ambiguity in service of a claim.
Parallel: Response painting or drawing series
Stay with the question, not the answer
Before writing the essay: make work in response to the question, not the position. What images, tensions, or forms does the question evoke before you've decided what you think?
  • ·
    What does "power" look like as a mark? What does "structural barrier" feel like as texture?
  • ·
    After writing: does the essay answer the question the paintings were asking? What do the paintings know that the essay doesn't?
Academic: Reflective essay
Structured critical reflection through a theoretical framework
Gibbs, Kolb, or Johns cycle — moving from experience through analysis to action plan.
Parallel: Studio response to the experience
Make before you analyse
Immediately after the experience you are reflecting on — before any analytical writing — make something in response to it. Let the studio work carry the emotional and somatic residue of the experience so the essay can think more clearly.
  • ·
    This is especially important in placement contexts — the studio becomes a decompression and processing space that protects both your wellbeing and the quality of your reflection
Academic: Case formulation
Clinical analysis: presenting problem → theory → intervention
A structured clinical document that maps a client presentation onto theoretical frameworks.
Parallel: Relational mark-making
What does the therapeutic relationship feel like as form?
After a session or placement day — not depicting the client, never identifying — make marks in response to the relational quality of the work. What did the therapeutic space feel like? Where was the tension? The warmth? The absence?
  • ·
    This is parallel to what Art Therapy training already formalises — but extended into your counselling and clinical placements too
  • ·
    Ethically: no identifying information, no case details. The work responds to affect and atmosphere, not narrative
Academic: Thesis / dissertation
A sustained scholarly argument across 15,000–100,000 words
The largest and longest piece of academic work — months or years of sustained engagement with a single question.
Parallel: The research body of work
A visual inquiry running the full length of the thesis
For each major phase of the thesis — literature review, fieldwork, analysis, writing — a corresponding studio period. The body of visual work that emerges becomes the parallel inquiry. At PhD level, this could constitute arts-based research data or a publishable creative component.
  • ·
    Document with dates so the creative work and the thesis can be read in relation to each other — what were you making while you were writing Chapter 3?
  • ·
    At PhD level: discuss with your supervisor early about whether the visual work could form a formal component of a practice-based thesis
04

By degree — how the practice evolves

The parallel practice doesn't start fresh with each degree. It deepens. What you make in the MSc year will still be in conversation with what you make in the PhD.

MSc · Disability Studies
Establishing the practice
Focus on: what does disability as social construct look and feel like as image? What does the medical model versus the social model produce in paint?

Consider: working in a consistent medium or format so the body of work is legible as a series.
MA · Counselling Psychology
The practice as reflexivity
The studio becomes your supervision space for your own internal process. What is evoked in you by therapeutic encounter? How does your positionality as a maker shape how you sit with clients?

The creative work feeds your reflexivity sections directly.
Diploma · Art Therapy
The practice becomes formal
Here the personal art-making journal is assessed. The parallel practice you've been sustaining informally now has an institutional form. Your years of practice mean you arrive here with genuine depth rather than starting from nothing.
PhD · Clinical / Public Health
The practice becomes methodology
At PhD level, the accumulated body of parallel work becomes arguable as arts-based research data. The practice that began as personal ritual can be theorised as a legitimate methodological choice. This is where Elson fully arrives.
05

Studio practice structures

The hardest part of a parallel practice is protecting it from the urgency of deadlines. These are structures that make it sustainable without being prescriptive.

▪ The assignment response session
Every time you begin a new assignment, spend one unstructured studio session before you do any reading or writing. The session's only rule: respond to the question or topic through making, without any analytical intent. Work freely. Date it. Keep it.
  • →
    Duration: 45 minutes to 2 hours. Not less — you need time to get past the first obvious response. Not more — this is a beginning, not a resolution.
  • →
    No critique during the session. The work doesn't need to be good. It needs to be honest.
▪ The weekly studio hour
One protected hour per week — in the calendar, immovable — that isn't tied to any specific assignment. This is the ambient studio space, the background hum of making that means you never fully stop. It accumulates across years.
  • →
    It can be as small as keeping a visual notebook in which you make marks, paste things, draw responses to readings. It doesn't require a studio.
  • →
    The commitment is to the time, not the outcome. Even if nothing emerges — especially if nothing emerges — document it.
▪ The submission ritual
After submitting each assignment — not before, not instead of — make a piece of work in direct response to it. Not a summary or illustration. A response. What do you now know that you couldn't know before writing it? What question did the assignment open that it couldn't close?
  • →
    This ritualises the transition between academic work and the next phase. It prevents the flattening of everything into deadlines.
  • →
    Over four degrees, this produces a body of work that traces your intellectual and personal development in a register that the academic work alone cannot.
▪ The placement decompression practice
For periods of clinical placement — MA, Diploma, PhD — the studio becomes a space for processing the emotional and relational residue of the work that cannot be carried into the next session. This is not art therapy for yourself. It is a professional practice of working through affect via material rather than allowing it to accumulate.
  • →
    Keep this separate from your formal placement portfolio. It is personal, private, and not assessed — though it will inform your reflexivity writing.
  • →
    Discuss with your supervisor the boundary between personal art-making as self-care and the formally assessed personal art journal. They are related but distinct.
06

Documentation — the parallel practice notebook

The notebook is what makes the practice scholarship rather than hobby. It doesn't need to be analytical as you go. It needs to be faithful — honest records that make later analysis possible.

What to record
  • 1
    Date, assignment context, durationWhat were you studying when you made this? How long did you work?
  • 2
    Materials usedMedium matters — the choice of paint over pencil is a decision that can be theorised
  • 3
    What you were thinking about when you beganThe prompt, the question, the anxiety, the concept — whatever brought you to the studio
  • 4
    What surprised you in the makingThe most important line in any entry — this is where the inquiry happens
  • 5
    Any connection to the academic workBrief and un-forced. Sometimes there's none yet. That's a valid entry too.
What not to force
  • ×
    Don't analyse the work as you goRecord what happened. Analysis comes later, when you can read across entries.
  • ×
    Don't judge whether it was goodThe quality of the making is not the point. The faithfulness of the documentation is.
  • ×
    Don't force a connection to the assignmentSometimes the creative work is asking a different question. Follow that — and note that the departure happened.
  • ×
    Don't skip documenting because the session felt unproductive"Nothing came. I sat with the materials for an hour. I kept thinking about X" is valuable data.
Photograph everything. Every piece, in good light, before it changes or disappears. Include a date in the filename. A decade from now, this archive is evidence of a sustained, rigorous, parallel intellectual practice — and at PhD level, it may become research data.
07

Prompt library

Starting prompts for studio sessions, organised by the type of academic work running alongside. Use these when the blank canvas is the hardest part. Ignore them when the work already knows where it's going.

For essays and critical writing
What does the concept I'm arguing about look like if it were a landscape?
Opening session
Make a mark for every time you felt uncertain while reading for this essay.
Research phase
What is the counter-argument to your thesis — painted, not written?
Mid-essay
What question does your conclusion refuse to ask?
Post-submission
For reflective writing
Make something in the first ten minutes after the experience you're reflecting on. Before words.
Immediately after
What colour is the feeling you're most reluctant to name in this reflection?
Emotional access
Where in your body did you feel the moment you're writing about? Work from there.
Somatic
What does "next time I would do differently" look like as form rather than plan?
Action phase
For placement and clinical work
Make marks for the quality of attention in the room today — not the content.
After placement
What remains when you strip away everything you were supposed to feel?
Countertransference
Where was the silence in the session? What does it weigh?
Therapeutic space
Make something for what you couldn't carry into the next session.
Decompression
For thesis phases
What drew you to this research question before you knew how to articulate it?
Beginning
Make a piece for the gap in the literature you're trying to fill.
Literature review
What are you most afraid your data will show?
Analysis phase
What does this thesis contribute — not to the field, but to you?
Completion
Over four degrees and perhaps a decade, these sessions accumulate into something that has no equivalent in any academic format — a visual record of what it felt like to think these thoughts, learn these things, and become whoever you become in the process of it. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, exactly what Rebecca Elson left behind.
