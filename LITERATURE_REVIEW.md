# 📖 Standalone Literature Review

> **A complete reference for MSc, MA, and PhD literature reviews**
> A literature review assessed as its own piece — separate from a thesis — is a thematic synthesis and critical argument, not an annotated list of sources. Your voice organises the literature. The literature does not organise you.

## 1. Introduction

> **Typical length:** 8–12% of total word count
> *(3,000-word review: ~300 words · 8,000-word review: ~700 words)*

The introduction establishes what you are reviewing, why it matters, how you searched, and how the review is organised. It does not summarise what you found — that comes later.

---

### 1.1 Scope and Purpose

Define precisely what body of literature you are reviewing and why. A literature review without a defined scope is unfocused; one without a stated purpose is merely a catalogue.

- State the topic, field, and the question or problem the review addresses
- Define any key terms that are contested or used differently across the literature
- Explain the boundaries of the review: time range, geographic scope, discipline, language

> 💡 **Key question:** What is the review *for*? Is it mapping a field, justifying a methodological choice, establishing a gap, or evaluating evidence for a practice recommendation? The purpose determines the structure.

---

### 1.2 Search Strategy

For an academic literature review, documenting how you searched is part of the scholarly transparency of the piece — even in a narrative (non-systematic) review.

- Name the databases searched: PsycINFO, PubMed, CINAHL, Scopus, JSTOR, Social Care Online — use those appropriate to your field
- State the key search terms and any Boolean logic used
- Note inclusion and exclusion criteria: what did you include and why? What did you exclude?
- State the date range of the search

> 📝 **Tip:** For a narrative review, one paragraph on search strategy is sufficient. For anything claiming to be systematic, refer to the [Systematic Review guide](SYSTEMATIC_REVIEW.md) — the protocol requirements are substantially more rigorous.

---

### 1.3 Organising Principle

Tell the reader how the review is structured and why. The choice of organising principle is itself an intellectual decision that should be defended, not just stated.

| Organising principle | When it works |
|---------------------|--------------|
| **Thematic** | The field contains distinct sub-debates or topic areas that can be analysed separately — the most common and usually the strongest choice |
| **Methodological** | You are evaluating how different research designs have addressed the same question — useful in health and psychology fields |
| **Chronological** | The development of the field *over time* is itself the argument — use sparingly |
| **Theoretical** | Different theoretical frameworks produce different findings — useful for contested or paradigm-driven fields |

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Organising by author or study ("Smith found X; Jones found Y; Brown found Z"). This produces an annotated bibliography, not a synthesis.

## 2. Theoretical Framework

> **Typical length:** 10–15% of total word count

Name the theoretical lens through which you are reading the literature. This shapes what you notice, what questions you ask of the evidence, and how you evaluate the field's assumptions. In social science and health fields, this section often carries significant analytical weight.

---

### 2.1 Naming and Explaining the Framework

- Identify the theory or theories explicitly — do not assume the reader shares your frame
- Explain the framework's core claims in your own words — citing the foundational theorists
- Justify why this framework is appropriate for the question you are reviewing
- If using multiple frameworks, explain how they relate: are they complementary, in tension, or operating at different levels of analysis?

> 📝 **Tip:** The theoretical framework is not a history of the theory. It is a working tool. Focus on the aspects of the theory that are analytically useful for your review — not a comprehensive account of everything the theory claims.

---

### 2.2 Applying the Framework to the Literature

Your framework should actively shape how you read and evaluate the sources you review — not just appear in this section and disappear.

- Return to the framework in your thematic sections: "From a social model perspective, this finding suggests..."
- Use the framework to identify what the literature assumes but does not examine
- Be honest when the literature you are reviewing does not use your framework — this is often itself a finding

---

## 3. Thematic Sections

> **Typical length:** 50–60% of total word count
> *Usually 3–5 themes, each with its own subsection*

This is the heart of the review. Each thematic section synthesises a cluster of literature around a finding, debate, or pattern — not around individual authors or studies.

---

### 3.1 What Each Theme Section Must Do

1. **Open with a topic sentence** that names the theme and states what the literature shows overall
2. **Synthesise across sources** — group studies by what they find, not by who wrote them
3. **Note agreements and patterns** — where does the evidence converge?
4. **Note disagreements and tensions** — where does it diverge, and why?
5. **Evaluate quality** — not all studies are equal; note methodological strengths and limitations
6. **Connect to your framework** — how does this theme look through your theoretical lens?
7. **Close with a transitional sentence** — what does this theme set up for the next?

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Paragraphs that alternate between study A and study B without ever synthesising them. Synthesis means showing *what they collectively reveal* — not what each one says in sequence.

---

### 3.2 Evaluating the Evidence

Critical evaluation does not mean finding fault with everything. It means reading the evidence carefully and honestly.

| Question to ask of each source | Why it matters |
|-------------------------------|---------------|
| Who is the sample, and how large? | Determines generalisability |
| What method was used, and is it appropriate? | Determines what kinds of claims are warranted |
| What are the authors' stated limitations? | Often signals where the field's knowledge ends |
| When was this published? | Older studies may have been superseded |
| Is there a conflict of interest? | Industry-funded research warrants extra scrutiny |
| Does this finding replicate? | Single studies are weaker evidence than replicated findings |

---

## 4. Critical Evaluation

> **Typically woven throughout the thematic sections, with a synthesising paragraph at the end**

A standalone critical evaluation section (or subsection at the end of the thematic body) draws together the patterns, limitations, and contested ground you have identified across the review.

---

### 4.1 Patterns Across the Literature

- What are the strongest areas of evidence — where does the field speak with confidence?
- What are the weakest — where is evidence sparse, contradictory, or methodologically limited?
- Are there populations, contexts, or questions that the literature consistently underrepresents?
- Are there theoretical assumptions the field makes without examining?

---

### 4.2 Methodological Patterns

- Does the field rely heavily on one methodology? What does this make invisible?
- Are quantitative and qualitative findings integrated, or do they occupy separate literatures?
- Are there differences in findings across methods — and if so, what do they tell us?

> 📝 **Tip:** Noting that most studies in your field are quantitative, or are conducted in Western, educated, industrial, rich, democratic (WEIRD) populations, is itself a substantive critical contribution — it locates where the field's assumptions are built in.

---

## 5. Gap Statement

> **Typical length:** 5–10% of total word count
> *The most important section — the logical destination of everything above*

The gap statement is not an afterthought. It is the argumentative conclusion of the review. Based on everything you have mapped and evaluated, you now identify precisely what the field has not done — and why that matters.

---

### 5.1 Writing the Gap Statement

- The gap should follow *directly* from your critical evaluation — it should feel inevitable, not bolted on
- Be precise: a gap is not "no one has studied this" but "this mechanism has not been examined in this population, using this method, within this theoretical frame"
- State why the gap matters — what are the consequences of this absence for theory, practice, or policy?
- If the review is supporting a research proposal, the gap statement leads directly to your research question

> 💡 **Key question:** Does your gap statement make the reader think *"of course — this is exactly what the field needs"*? If it feels like an arbitrary criticism rather than a logical conclusion, return to your critical evaluation and look for what you have not yet named.

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Gaps framed as "no one has studied X in Y city" or "the literature lacks a study using my exact method." Geographic novelty and methodological novelty are not intellectual contributions on their own. The gap must matter theoretically or practically.

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Recommended Process

| Order | Step | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | **Define scope and question** | Prevents unmanageable breadth |
| 2 | **Search and screen literature** | Systematic before selective |
| 3 | **Read and annotate sources** | Build your synthesis matrix as you go |
| 4 | **Identify themes** | Cluster by what the literature shows, not by author |
| 5 | **Draft thematic sections** | Write the synthesis, not the summary |
| 6 | **Draft critical evaluation** | What patterns have you found across the whole? |
| 7 | **Draft gap statement** | The logical conclusion of what you have mapped |
| 8 | **Draft introduction** | Write last — you now know what the review contains |

### 📊 The Synthesis Matrix

Before writing, build a matrix: rows are sources, columns are themes, methodologies, populations, findings, and limitations. This makes patterns visible before you try to write them.

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Scope is clearly defined and justified in the introduction
- [ ] Search strategy is documented with databases, terms, and inclusion/exclusion criteria
- [ ] Organising principle is stated and justified
- [ ] Theoretical framework is named, explained, and applied throughout
- [ ] Thematic sections synthesise across sources — not source-by-source summaries
- [ ] Evidence is evaluated critically, not just reported
- [ ] Agreements and disagreements in the literature are both addressed
- [ ] Methodological limitations of the field are identified
- [ ] Gap statement follows logically from the critical evaluation
- [ ] Gap is precise, significant, and theoretically or practically justified
- [ ] All sources cited correctly in APA 7th
- [ ] Word count is within the permitted range

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
The standalone literature review at MSc level is often a preparatory assignment for the thesis. Treat it as the foundation for your thesis literature review — the themes you identify here will shape your research question.

#### MA (e.g. Counselling Psychology)
Reviews in counselling and psychology often need to navigate the tension between quantitative (RCT) evidence hierarchies and qualitative evidence about lived experience. Your theoretical framework section is the place to address this tension explicitly.

#### PhD
At PhD level, your literature review may be expected to be original in its synthesis — not just a map of the field, but a reframing of it. The gap statement should point toward a contribution that is genuinely new.
