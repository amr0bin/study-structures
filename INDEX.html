Core written work

5 types · all degrees

These appear in every postgraduate degree. Getting the structure of each one internalised early means you can put all your energy into content rather than form.

Thesis / Dissertation
The centrepiece of every postgraduate degree. A sustained, original scholarly argument built on a clear research question, literature synthesis, methodology, findings, and discussion. The structure is consistent across levels — what scales is depth, word count, and the degree of originality expected.
All degrees MSc 15–20k · MA 15–20k · Dip 10–15k · PhD 60–100k
📄 Full structure guide →
  1. Introduction
    Rationale · structure overview · research question
  2. Literature review
    Theoretical framework · state of the art · gap statement
  3. Methodology
    Design · method · justification · limitations
  4. Results
    Objective presentation of findings matched to research question
  5. Discussion
    Interpretation linked to literature · implications stated
  6. Conclusion
    Answer to research question · contributions · future directions
Critical / Analytical Essay
The most frequent assignment type across all taught modules. An argument-driven response to a set question or prompt. Unlike the thesis, it does not require primary research — but it must engage critically with the literature and construct a clear, evidenced position.
All degrees 1,500–5,000 words depending on level
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Introduction
    Context · your argument (thesis statement) · roadmap
  2. Main body paragraphs
    Each paragraph = one claim + evidence + analysis + link to argument
  3. Counter-argument
    Acknowledge the strongest opposing view · rebut it
  4. Conclusion
    Restate argument · synthesise evidence · wider implications
The thesis statement (your argument) should appear in your introduction and be answerable. Every paragraph should connect back to it — if it doesn't, cut it.
Standalone Literature Review
Sometimes assessed as its own piece — separate from a thesis. A thematic synthesis of existing research that maps what is known, what is contested, and where the gaps lie. The key distinction from a summary: your voice organises the literature, not the other way around.
MSc MA PhD 3,000–8,000 words
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Introduction
    Scope · search strategy · organising principle
  2. Theoretical framework
    The lens through which you're reading the literature
  3. Thematic sections
    Group studies by what they show — not chronologically
  4. Critical evaluation
    Quality, limitations, contestations in the field
  5. Gap statement
    What is missing · what your or future research could address
Research Proposal
Required for admission to every degree you're entering, and sometimes assessed within a programme. Essentially a mini thesis structure: you demonstrate that you have a viable, well-grounded question and a credible plan to answer it. The PhD version is the most substantial.
All degrees Taught: 1,000–3,000 · PhD application: 5,000–10,000
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Title & rationale
    Why this, why now, why you
  2. Research question
    Specific, answerable, appropriately scoped
  3. Literature context & gap
    What exists · what's missing · your contribution
  4. Proposed methodology
    Design · method · participants · analysis
  5. Ethical considerations
    Consent · risk · access · positionality
  6. Timeline
    Realistic phased plan across the degree period
For PhD proposals specifically: supervisors are looking for evidence that you can think independently about a problem. Show you understand the existing debates — don't just describe the topic.
Annotated Bibliography
A list of sources, each followed by a brief critical annotation. Often assigned early in a programme to develop your ability to evaluate literature. Each annotation summarises the source and then critically assesses its relevance, methodology, and limitations.
MSc MA PhD 150–300 words per source · typically 8–15 sources
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Full reference
    In your required citation style (usually APA 7th)
  2. Summary
    What the source argues or reports — 2–3 sentences
  3. Critical evaluation
    Strengths, limitations, methodological notes
  4. Relevance
    Why this source matters to your research question or topic

Applied & case work

2 types · MSc · MA · PhD

These apply theory directly to a real or constructed scenario — a policy, organisation, person, or client. The structure shifts significantly depending on your field.

Case Study — Social Science / Policy
An in-depth analysis of a person, organisation, community, policy, or event, examined through a theoretical lens. Common in Disability Studies for analysing policy frameworks or lived experience accounts. The goal is to generate insight that extends beyond the single case.
MSc PhD 2,000–5,000 words
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Introduction
    The case · your analytical question · the theoretical lens
  2. Context
    Background needed to understand the case
  3. Analysis
    Apply the theoretical framework to the case data
  4. Discussion
    What does this case reveal beyond itself?
  5. Conclusion
    Implications · limitations of this single-case approach
Case Formulation — Clinical / Counselling
A structured clinical analysis of a real or simulated client — integrating presenting concerns, developmental history, psychological theory, and a planned intervention. This is a core professional competency in both Counselling Psychology and Clinical Psych, and it has a very specific structure.
MA PhD 2,000–4,000 words
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Presenting problem
    What the client brings · in their own terms
  2. Developmental & social history
    Relevant background · predisposing factors
  3. Theoretical formulation
    How your chosen framework explains the presentation
  4. Precipitating & perpetuating factors
    What triggered it · what maintains it
  5. Protective factors & strengths
    Resources the client brings
  6. Intervention plan
    Goals · modality · approach · rationale
  7. Risk assessment
    To self / others · protective factors · management plan
In Counselling Psych, your positionality as the therapist is always part of the formulation. Reflexivity — how your own background might affect the therapeutic relationship — is not optional.

Reflective writing

2 types · MA · Diploma · PhD

Reflective writing in postgraduate study is not journalling — it has a formal structure and is assessed on the quality of critical analysis, not personal disclosure. It is central to both Counselling Psychology and Art Therapy.

Reflective Essay
A structured critical examination of an experience, encounter, or piece of practice — connecting what happened to theory and professional development. Most programmes specify a framework (Gibbs, Kolb, Johns). The essay moves between the personal and the analytical throughout.
MA Diploma PhD 1,500–3,000 words
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Description
    What happened? Factual account — no interpretation yet
  2. Feelings
    What were you thinking and feeling? Honest, specific
  3. Evaluation
    What was good and bad about the experience?
  4. Analysis
    What sense can you make of the situation? Link to theory
  5. Conclusion
    What else could you have done? What have you learned?
  6. Action plan
    If this arose again, what would you do differently?
The most common mistake is staying in "description" mode throughout. Examiners are looking for depth in the Analysis stage — that is where theory meets experience, and where the marks live.
Personal Development Log / Process Journal
An ongoing, cumulative reflective document maintained throughout a placement or training year. In Counselling Psychology it tracks your development as a practitioner; in Art Therapy it documents your personal art-making process and its therapeutic significance. Not a diary — it is critically reflective throughout.
MA Diploma Ongoing · reviewed at intervals or at year end
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Experience / observation
    What occurred — specific and concrete
  2. Emotional response
    Your affective reaction — named precisely
  3. Theoretical connection
    Which framework or concept illuminates this?
  4. Implications for practice
    What does this mean for how you work?
  5. Supervision link
    What will you bring to supervision? What support do you need?

Practice portfolios

2 types · MA · Diploma

Portfolios are not single assignments — they are accumulated evidence of your professional development, built across a full placement year. Starting them well-structured from day one saves enormous stress at submission.

Professional Practice Portfolio — Counselling Psychology
A comprehensive, evidence-based record of your clinical training. Submitted at the end of a placement year, it demonstrates competency across ethical practice, theoretical application, client work, and personal development. Every section needs evidence — not just description.
MA only Typically 10,000–15,000 words + appendices
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Placement log
    Hours, client demographics, session counts — verified by supervisor
  2. Process notes or session recordings
    With client consent · anonymised
  3. Case study (1–2 clients)
    Full formulation with theory-practice integration
  4. Supervisor reports
    Mid-placement and end-of-placement assessment
  5. Personal development review
    How you have grown · areas still developing
  6. Ethical incidents log
    Challenges encountered and how you navigated them
Keep a running document throughout placement rather than trying to reconstruct it at the end. A strong portfolio feels like it was lived in, not assembled in a panic.
Practice Portfolio — Art Therapy Diploma
Documents your clinical practice across art therapy placements alongside your own personal art-making. The unique element is the dual evidence base — client artwork and your own creative process are both assessed. Strong documentation of client work requires careful ethical attention (consent, anonymisation, storage of artwork).
Diploma only Typically 8,000–12,000 words + visual documentation
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Placement log
    Hours, client groups, setting type · supervisor verified
  2. Client artwork documentation
    Photographs with session notes · anonymised · consent obtained
  3. Session process notes
    What happened in the room · art-making process · themes
  4. Personal art journal
    Your own art-making — visual + written reflection
  5. Supervisor reports
    Assessment of clinical skills and professional development
  6. Ethical reflections
    Boundary issues, dual role challenges, consent processes

Creative practice with commentary

1 type · Diploma only

Unique to the Art Therapy Diploma. Your own art-making is not incidental to training — it is a core assessed component, treated as both personal development and theoretical inquiry.

Personal Art-Making with Written Commentary
You engage in your own regular art-making practice throughout the Diploma — not as entertainment but as a reflective and therapeutic process. This is documented in a visual journal (the artworks themselves) and accompanied by written commentary that connects your creative process to therapeutic theory. Examiners assess both the quality of your reflection and the honesty of your engagement with the material.
Diploma only Ongoing visual journal + 2,000–4,000 words commentary
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Description of the art-making process
    What media · what emerged · what surprised you
  2. Emotional and somatic response
    What arose during making · bodily and affective experience
  3. Theoretical connection
    Which therapeutic frameworks illuminate your process?
  4. Link to clinical practice
    How does experiencing this yourself inform how you hold it for clients?
  5. Development over time
    How has your relationship to your own art-making shifted across training?
The most common feedback is that students intellectualise rather than genuinely engage. The commentary should be honest about difficulty, resistance, and what the process stirred up — not just a tidy interpretation of a finished image.

Advanced research work

2 types · MSc · PhD

These formats are more methodologically rigorous and appear in programmes with a strong research training component. The systematic review in particular is a distinct skill from the literature review — it has its own formal protocol.

Systematic Review
Fundamentally different from a narrative literature review. It is a formal research methodology with a pre-registered protocol, explicit search strategy, structured screening process (PRISMA), and quality appraisal tools. The goal is to answer a specific question by comprehensively and transparently synthesising all available evidence on a topic.
MSc PhD 8,000–15,000 words · often a substantial standalone piece
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Protocol & registration
    Pre-register your question, eligibility criteria, and methods (PROSPERO)
  2. Search strategy
    Databases searched · search terms · date ranges · limits
  3. PRISMA screening
    Records identified → screened → eligible → included (with flowchart)
  4. Quality appraisal
    Assess risk of bias in each included study (CASP, Cochrane, etc.)
  5. Data extraction
    Systematic extraction of relevant data from each study
  6. Synthesis
    Narrative synthesis or meta-analysis · GRADE if applicable
  7. Discussion & limitations
    What the evidence shows · gaps · quality caveats
Empirical Journal-Format Paper
Some MSc programmes and most PhD programmes require you to write up research in the format of a peer-reviewed journal article — usually APA style. This is distinct from a thesis chapter: it is tightly wordcounted, structured for journal submission, and written for a specialist audience rather than an examiner. PhD candidates often submit these as part of a "papers model" thesis.
MSc PhD 5,000–8,000 words · formatted to target journal guidelines
📄 Guide coming soon
  1. Abstract
    150–250 words · background, aim, method, results, conclusion
  2. Introduction
    Literature context · gap · hypotheses or aims · tight and focused
  3. Method
    Participants · materials · procedure · analysis · ethics
  4. Results
    Statistical or thematic findings · tables / figures · no interpretation
  5. Discussion
    Interpretation · links to literature · limitations · future research
  6. References
    APA 7th · complete and consistent

Examinations & viva voce

2 types · MSc · PhD

Formal assessments under conditions. The written exam rewards preparation of clear, structured arguments under time pressure. The PhD viva is an oral defence of your entire thesis — the most intellectually demanding moment of doctoral study.

Written Examination
Essay-based exams under timed conditions, either unseen (question revealed on the day) or seen (question released in advance). At postgraduate level, the marker is looking for the same qualities as in a coursework essay: a clear argument, critical engagement with theory, and evidenced claims. Time management is the main additional challenge.
MSc MA Usually 2–3 hours · 1–3 essay questions
📄 Exam prep guide coming soon
  1. Read all questions first (5 min)
    Choose carefully · note which questions allow you to show your best thinking
  2. Plan before writing (8–10 min per essay)
    Argument · 3–4 main points · key evidence for each
  3. Write to time
    Divide remaining time equally across essays · do not over-run the first
  4. Reserve 5 min to review
    Check your argument is clear · introductions say what you'll argue
A partial answer to all questions always outperforms a perfect answer to one. Markers cannot award marks for essays not written.
Viva Voce — PhD Oral Defence
A 1–3 hour oral examination of your completed thesis, conducted by two examiners (usually one internal, one external). You are expected to defend every choice — methodological, theoretical, analytical — and to situate your contribution within the field. Most students pass; the goal is not to catch you out but to verify that the work is genuinely yours and that you understand it deeply.
PhD only 1–3 hours · 2 examiners · typically minor or major corrections follow
📄 Viva preparation guide coming soon
  1. Opening question
    Usually: "In your own words, what is your thesis about and what does it contribute?"
  2. Deep dive into the research question
    Why this question · why now · what alternatives did you consider?
  3. Methodology scrutiny
    Defend every design decision · what would you do differently?
  4. Literature engagement
    Why did you include / exclude certain works · how do you respond to key critics?
  5. Contribution and implications
    What does the field now know that it didn't? · What are the next steps?
  6. Corrections discussion
    Examiners outline any required changes · usually minor or major corrections
Re-read your thesis in the weeks before the viva as if you are a critical examiner. Note every place where you feel uncertain or where a question could be asked — those are exactly the areas to prepare.
