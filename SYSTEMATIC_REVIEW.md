# 🔬 Systematic Review

> **A complete reference for MSc and PhD systematic reviews**
> A systematic review is a formal research methodology — not a thorough literature review. It answers a specific question by comprehensively, transparently, and reproducibly synthesising all available evidence on that question. The key word is *systematic*: every decision is documented and defensible.

## The Difference Between a Systematic and a Narrative Review

Before beginning, be clear which type you are conducting. Many postgraduate assignments ask for a narrative review (see [Literature Review guide](LITERATURE_REVIEW.md)). A systematic review has specific methodological requirements that a narrative review does not.

| Feature | Systematic review | Narrative review |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| Protocol | Pre-registered before searching | Not required |
| Search | Exhaustive across multiple databases | Selective and representative |
| Screening | Documented with explicit criteria | Implicit |
| Quality appraisal | Formal tool applied to all studies | Informal and variable |
| Synthesis | Structured; meta-analysis if appropriate | Thematic; interpretive |
| Reproducibility | Another researcher could replicate it | Not expected |
| PRISMA flowchart | Required | Not required |

> 💡 **Key question:** Does your assignment brief explicitly ask for a *systematic* review? If it asks for a "literature review" or "critical review," refer to the [Literature Review guide](LITERATURE_REVIEW.md) instead. Starting a full systematic review when a narrative review is required wastes significant time.

## 1. Protocol & Registration

> **Completed before any searching begins**

A systematic review protocol specifies, in advance, what you will do — so that your decisions are not unconsciously shaped by what you find. Pre-registration prevents the moving of goalposts after results are known.

---

### 1.1 The PICO(S) Framework

Most systematic review questions are structured using PICO(S):

| Element | Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **P** | Population | Adults with acquired brain injury |
| **I** | Intervention (or exposure) | Art therapy |
| **C** | Comparator | Treatment as usual / waitlist |
| **O** | Outcome | Quality of life; psychological wellbeing |
| **S** | Study design (optional) | RCTs; controlled trials |

Your review question should be answerable by the evidence that exists — not so specific that no studies qualify, not so broad that thousands do.

---

### 1.2 Pre-registration

Register your protocol on **PROSPERO** (for health and social care systematic reviews) before beginning your search. PROSPERO is free and the registration process requires you to specify:

- The review question (in PICO format)
- Inclusion and exclusion criteria
- Databases to be searched
- Search terms
- Screening procedure
- Quality appraisal tool
- Planned synthesis approach

> 📝 **Tip:** For student systematic reviews, your institution may not require PROSPERO registration — but completing the PROSPERO registration fields as a planning exercise is valuable regardless, as it forces every methodological decision to be made before bias can enter.

---

### 1.3 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

State explicitly what studies you will include and exclude, and why. Criteria typically cover:

- **Population:** who must participants be?
- **Intervention / exposure:** what must the study examine?
- **Comparator:** what comparators are acceptable?
- **Outcomes:** what outcomes must be reported?
- **Study design:** what designs will you include? (RCTs only? Including qualitative?)
- **Publication characteristics:** date range, language, publication type (peer-reviewed only?)

## 2. Search Strategy

> **Comprehensive, documented, and reproducible**

The search must be designed to capture all relevant studies — not just the ones that are easy to find. An underpowered search is a methodological flaw that undermines the review's validity.

---

### 2.1 Databases

Search multiple databases appropriate to your field. For health and social science:

| Database | Coverage |
|---------|---------|
| **MEDLINE / PubMed** | Medicine and biomedical sciences |
| **PsycINFO** | Psychology and mental health |
| **CINAHL** | Nursing and allied health |
| **Embase** | Biomedical and pharmacological |
| **Cochrane Library** | Existing systematic reviews and RCTs |
| **Social Care Online** | UK social care evidence |
| **JSTOR / Scopus / Web of Science** | Broader social science and humanities |

Search at least three databases. Document the search date for each.

---

### 2.2 Search Terms

Build a search string using:

- **Keywords** and **MeSH terms** (controlled vocabulary) for each PICO element
- **Boolean operators:** AND (combines elements), OR (combines synonyms), NOT (excludes)
- **Truncation:** using * to capture variants (e.g., therap* captures therapy, therapist, therapeutic)
- **Phrase searching:** quotation marks for exact phrases ("art therapy")

> 📝 **Tip:** Run your search string past a subject librarian before committing to it. A poorly constructed search string is one of the most common reasons systematic reviews fail to find relevant evidence.

---

### 2.3 Additional Sources

Beyond database searching:

- **Hand-searching:** check reference lists of all included studies for additional eligible sources
- **Grey literature:** for some questions, government reports, policy documents, or unpublished studies are relevant
- **Citation tracking:** use Google Scholar to find studies citing key included papers

---

## 3. PRISMA Screening

> **Document every decision with a PRISMA flowchart**

PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) is the international reporting standard. The PRISMA flowchart shows exactly how many records were identified, screened, assessed for eligibility, and included — and why studies were excluded at each stage.

---

### 3.1 Screening Stages

| Stage | What happens |
|-------|-------------|
| **Identification** | Total records retrieved from all databases (before deduplication) |
| **Deduplication** | Remove duplicate records of the same study |
| **Title and abstract screening** | Screen all remaining records against inclusion criteria — exclude those clearly ineligible |
| **Full-text review** | Retrieve full text of potentially eligible studies; apply criteria rigorously |
| **Inclusion** | Final list of included studies |

At each exclusion stage, record the reason for exclusion. If conducting a rigorous review, two independent reviewers screen each study and resolve disagreements by discussion or third-party adjudication.

---

### 3.2 The PRISMA Flowchart

Your submission should include a PRISMA flowchart showing:

- Records identified (per database)
- Records after deduplication
- Records screened (title/abstract)
- Records excluded (with n and reasons)
- Full texts assessed for eligibility
- Full texts excluded (with n and reasons)
- Studies included in the review

The updated PRISMA 2020 template is available at [prisma-statement.org](http://prisma-statement.org).

## 4. Quality Appraisal

> **Assess risk of bias in every included study**

Not all studies are equal in methodological quality. Quality appraisal assesses the risk that a study's findings are distorted by design flaws, bias, or reporting limitations.

---

### 4.1 Choosing an Appraisal Tool

Use the tool appropriate to the study designs in your review:

| Study design | Recommended tool |
|-------------|-----------------|
| RCTs | Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool (RoB 2) |
| Non-randomised studies | ROBINS-I |
| Qualitative studies | CASP Qualitative Checklist |
| Mixed-methods | Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT) |
| Any study type | CASP tools (various designs) |

Apply the tool consistently to every included study. Present appraisal results in a table.

---

### 4.2 What Quality Appraisal Affects

- **Confidence in findings:** high-quality evidence warrants stronger conclusions than low-quality evidence
- **Weighting in synthesis:** in narrative synthesis, quality appraisal informs how prominently you rely on a study's findings
- **GRADE assessment:** if using GRADE (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development and Evaluations), quality appraisal determines the overall certainty of the evidence

---

## 5. Data Extraction

> **Systematic and consistent across all included studies**

Data extraction captures, in a standardised format, the information you need from each included study to answer your review question and to characterise the evidence base.

---

### 5.1 What to Extract

Develop a data extraction form before beginning. Standard fields include:

- Author(s), year, country
- Study design
- Sample: size, characteristics, inclusion criteria
- Intervention or exposure: description, duration, comparator
- Outcomes: measures used, results
- Funding source
- Risk of bias rating (from quality appraisal)
- Key findings relevant to your review question

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Extracting data without a pre-specified form, or extracting different information from different studies. Inconsistent extraction produces an inconsistent synthesis.

---

## 6. Synthesis

> **Narrative synthesis or meta-analysis, depending on the evidence**

---

### 6.1 When to Use Meta-Analysis

Meta-analysis statistically pools the results of multiple studies to produce an overall effect estimate. It is appropriate when:

- Studies use the same or similar outcomes measured in compatible ways
- Studies are sufficiently similar in design, population, and intervention (low heterogeneity)
- There are enough studies (generally, at least 5–10) to pool

If in doubt, or if studies are too heterogeneous to pool, use narrative synthesis.

---

### 6.2 Narrative Synthesis

Narrative synthesis organises and interprets findings without statistical pooling. It should still be systematic:

- Group studies by their findings, outcomes, or sub-questions
- Describe convergent findings and divergent findings separately
- Relate findings to study quality — weight high-quality evidence more heavily in your conclusions
- Use tables and summary matrices to present findings transparently

---

### 6.3 GRADE (if required)

GRADE assesses the overall certainty of the evidence body (not individual studies) across four levels: High, Moderate, Low, Very Low. It accounts for risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

---

## 7. Discussion & Limitations

> **Typical length:** 15–20% of total word count

---

### 7.1 What the Evidence Shows

- What are the main findings across the included studies?
- How confident are you in these findings, given the quality of the evidence?
- What are the clinical, policy, or theoretical implications?

---

### 7.2 Limitations of the Review

Every systematic review has limitations — acknowledge them:

- **Search limitations:** were any databases or grey literature sources not searched?
- **Language bias:** excluding non-English studies may skew findings
- **Publication bias:** unpublished studies (often with negative or null results) are harder to find
- **Heterogeneity:** if studies varied substantially, pooled findings should be interpreted cautiously
- **Time limitations:** if the review was conducted within a degree programme, it may not have been possible to conduct the full double-screening and appraisal process

## Writing Process & Strategy

### ✍️ Timeline

A rigorous systematic review takes significantly longer than other assignment types. For a postgraduate programme systematic review:

| Phase | Approximate time |
|-------|----------------|
| Protocol development and registration | 2–3 weeks |
| Searching | 1–2 weeks |
| Title/abstract screening | 2–3 weeks |
| Full-text screening | 2–3 weeks |
| Data extraction and quality appraisal | 3–4 weeks |
| Synthesis and write-up | 4–6 weeks |

Start earlier than you think you need to.

### 📊 Tools

- **Reference management:** Zotero or Endnote (essential for deduplication and citation management)
- **Screening:** Rayyan (free, web-based) or Covidence (paid) for title/abstract screening
- **Data extraction:** a spreadsheet (Excel or Google Sheets) with a pre-specified form
- **Quality appraisal:** download the relevant tool (CASP, Cochrane, ROBINS-I) and apply consistently

---

### ✅ Submission Checklist

- [ ] Review question stated in PICO(S) format
- [ ] Protocol registered (PROSPERO) or documented before searching began
- [ ] Inclusion and exclusion criteria specified explicitly
- [ ] Multiple databases searched and documented with search strings and dates
- [ ] Additional searching (reference lists, grey literature) conducted where appropriate
- [ ] PRISMA flowchart included showing all stages of screening
- [ ] Reasons for exclusion at full-text stage documented
- [ ] Quality appraisal conducted using appropriate tool for each study design
- [ ] Appraisal results presented in a table
- [ ] Data extraction conducted systematically using a pre-specified form
- [ ] Synthesis is structured — not narrative summary study by study
- [ ] Evidence quality is reflected in the confidence of conclusions
- [ ] Limitations of the review are acknowledged
- [ ] PRISMA reporting checklist completed (available at prisma-statement.org)
- [ ] All citations in APA 7th

---

### 🎓 Notes for Specific Degrees

#### MSc (e.g. Disability Studies)
At MSc level, a systematic review assignment may require a simplified version of the full protocol — typically without mandatory PROSPERO registration and sometimes with a single screener. Follow your assignment brief carefully and document every decision even if the full protocol is not required.

#### PhD
At PhD level, a systematic review may form a complete chapter of your thesis — or even the primary contribution of the thesis. In this context, full adherence to PRISMA, double-screening, and GRADE is expected. A Cochrane Review (the gold standard of systematic reviews) is a significant PhD-level contribution.
